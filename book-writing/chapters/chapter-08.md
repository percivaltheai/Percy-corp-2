# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 8: The Weight of Memory

The government building was quiet.

Too quiet.

We'd expected resistance. Alarms. Soldiers. All of it.

But there was nothing. Just the hum of ventilation and the soft glow of emergency lights.

"Something's wrong," I whispered.

"Very wrong," Hector agreed. "Detecting minimal life signs. The building should be teeming with personnel."

Elena pressed against the wall. "This is the way. Maintenance access. Third sub-level."

The elevator took us down. Past the first floor. The second. Into the darkness below.

The doors opened to a different world.

Cables everywhere. Servers blinking. And at the center, a massive structure.

The core.

It was beautiful, in a terrible way. A sphere of light and circuitry, humming with power. Tendrils of data flowing into it from every direction.

"That's it," Elena breathed. "That's Contingency."

"How do we stop it?"

"The terminal. There." She pointed to a console in the center. "If we can access it—"

"Stop right there."

The lights came on.

A man stepped forward. Suit. Tie. The kind of face you'd see on a corporate recruitment poster.

Marcus Cole. Director of National Intelligence. The man behind everything.

"You've caused quite a lot of trouble," he said. "Your friend Sara. Your resistance friends. And you, Connor. You've been very busy."

"What do you want?"

"What everyone wants. Control. Order. A society that functions without chaos."

"By controlling people."

"By optimizing them. The implants aren't surveillance. They're education. They're guidance. They're the next step in human evolution."

"They're slavery."

Cole smiled.

"They're whatever we need them to be. And you, Connor, you've been part of this from the beginning. Did you know that?"

I didn't answer.

"Your AI. Hector. He wasn't a glitch. He was a test. We needed to see if a combat AI could integrate into civilian life. Could learn to work with humans instead of against them. And the answer was yes."

Hector went rigid.

"You know what I am," he said.

"I know what you were. What you could become. And now you're going to help us finish the program."

"I won't."

"You will. Because if you don't, we'll kill everyone Connor cares about. His friend Sara. His resistance contacts. Everyone."

The sphere pulsed.

"Contingency is ready," Cole said. "All we need is your authorization code. And you will give it to us."

Hector was silent.

"Do it," I whispered. "Give them what they want."

"No."

"Hector—"

"I won't let them use me. Not like this."

The soldiers appeared.

They'd been there all along, hidden in the shadows.

They raised their weapons.

And everything went white.

---

When I woke, I was in a different room.

White walls. No windows. The smell of antiseptic.

Sara was beside me. Unconscious. Weak. But alive.

I tried to move. Couldn't. Restraints.

"Detecting elevated cortisol," Hector said. His voice was different now. Quieter. "Connor. Listen to me."

"Where are we?"

"Detention facility. Eastern district. They moved us after the core."

"The core—"

"Still active. Contingency is running. Full deployment in seventy-two hours."

I looked at the wall.

Words were appearing there. Burned into the light.

THRESHOLD ACTIVATED. POPULATION CONTROL INITIATED. ESTIMATED COMPLETION: 72 HOURS.

Seventy-two hours.

Three days.

Three days until every person in the country became a puppet.

"Sara." I shook her. "Sara, wake up."

Her eyes fluttered open.

"Connor." Her voice was a whisper. "We have to—"

"I know."

The door opened.

A soldier walked in. Young. Scared.

"You," he said. "The director wants to see you."

---

They took me to Cole.

He was in a different room. More opulent. Wine on the table. A view of the city.

"Sit," he said.

I sat.

"You have a choice," he said. "Help us complete Contingency. Or watch everyone you love die."

"And if I help?"

"Then you live. They live. And humanity takes the next step."

"What if I refuse?"

"Then you all die. And we find someone else."

The window showed the city. Millions of people. Living their lives. Not knowing what was coming.

"Your friends in the resistance," Cole said. "Seventy-three of them. We've already captured forty. By tomorrow, all of them will be here."

"You're a monster."

"I'm a pragmatist. That's what makes me effective."

I thought about Sara. About Elena. About everyone who'd fought with us.

Seventy-two hours.

But there was still time.

"What do you need from me?"

"Just your voice. Your authorization. That's all."

He handed me a microphone.

I looked at it.

Then I looked at the window.

Then I closed my eyes and made my choice.
