# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 6: Ghosts

The safe house was Marcus's idea.

Marcus was a ghost himself. Ex-military, ex-everything, living in the margins of the city where the cameras didn't reach. He'd owed Hector a favor for years, some debt from before my time, and now we were calling it in.

"This way," he said, leading us through a door that didn't look like a door. Behind it, a staircase descending into darkness.

The building had been a textile factory in its better days. Now it was just concrete and rust and shadows. But it was safe. That was what mattered.

"Third floor," Marcus said. "No windows. Concrete walls. They could bomb this place and you'd survive."

"Comforting," I muttered.

He wasn't kidding. The room was spartan. A mattress on the floor. A table with a laptop that looked older than I was. Not much else.

"Sara's beacon," Hector said. "I've been tracking it."

My heart stopped.

"She's alive?"

"Barely. She's being moved. They took her from the detention facility an hour ago."

"Where?"

"North. Toward the old industrial zone."

I grabbed the laptop. Started searching. Data streams, news feeds, anything that might help.

"Detecting elevated stress," Hector observed.

"Shut up."

The news was full of nothing. Local elections. Sports. Weather. But dig deeper, past the noise, and patterns emerged.

Disappearances. All over the city. People who'd accessed restricted files. People who'd asked questions. People who'd seen too much.

"Seventeen," I said.

"What?"

"Seventeen people have gone missing this week. All connected to the data breach."

"That's not possible," Sara said. But I could hear the fear in her voice.

"It's possible. It's happening. And we're next."

The laptop pinged.

An email. Unknown sender.

Subject: We know what you have. We know who you are. Meet us.

Below, an address. A time. No signature.

"Is this real?" I asked.

"I can't verify," Hector said. "But it's either resistance or trap. Fifty-fifty odds."

"Those aren't great odds."

"No. But they're better than the alternative."

Sara grabbed my arm. "We have to go."

"I know."

We left the safe house at midnight. The city was different at night. Quieter. More dangerous.

The address led to an old warehouse on the waterfront. The kind of place that had been converted to lofts for artists with more ambition than money.

Three people were waiting.

They looked ordinary. The kind of people you'd pass on the street. But their eyes were different. Aware. Watchful.

One stepped forward. A woman in her forties. Grey in her hair, lines on her face. But her eyes were sharp.

"Connor Sullivan," she said. "Sara spoke of you."

"Where is she?"

"Safe. For now. But we need to move fast. They've activated Threshold."

My blood went cold.

"What?"

"The program. The one you destroyed. It's not gone. It's been rebuilt. And they're activating it now."

The woman led us through a back entrance. Down a staircase that hadn't been used in decades. Into a basement that had been converted into something else entirely.

Screens covered the walls. Data streams flowed across them. And in the center, a holographic display of the city.

"Seventy-three people," the woman said. "That's how many they took last night. Just in this city."

"Taken where?"

"No one knows. They just disappear."

The holographic display pulsed. I could see dots moving on it. Red dots. Blue dots. Dots that appeared and disappeared.

"What is this?"

"Our network. The people who know. The ones fighting back. We've been tracking the implants. The data streams. Trying to figure out how Threshold works."

"And?"

"We don't know. But we know it's coming. And when it comes, everyone with an implant will be affected."

"Everyone."

"Every single person. Three hundred million people in this country alone. They become puppets. They do whatever they're told."

I thought about Derek. About Linda. About the people I worked with. About everyone I'd ever known.

They'd all be controlled.

"What can we do?"

The woman looked at me. Her eyes were tired. Old.

"We need to shut it down. At the source."

"Where's the source?"

"That's what we've been trying to find."

One of the screens flickered. A face appeared on it. Sara.

"She's okay," the woman said. "She's safe. But she needs you."

The screen showed Sara in a room. Bunk beds. Fluorescent lights. She looked tired, but alive.

"Connor!" She saw me on the screen. "Thank god. We have to—"

The screen went black.

"Detecting interference," Hector said. "They're tracing us. We need to move."

"How did they find us?"

"It doesn't matter." The woman was already moving. "They've been watching everyone. Every person who knows is a target. You're all connected."

"To what?"

"To the network. To Threshold. They built it into the implants. Into all of us."

We ran.

Through corridors. Up stairs. Out into the night.

The city was different now. The same streets, the same buildings. But the people were different. They walked with their phones in their hands, their implants buzzing with notifications.

They had no idea.

"What do we do?" I asked.

"We find the source," the woman said. "We find the server. The main hub where all the data comes together. And we destroy it."

"How?"

"That's where you come in."

She handed me a device. Small. Metallic.

"What is this?"

"A disruptor. It can shut down an individual implant. But it only works at close range."

"One person at a time."

"One person at a time. And there are millions of them."

The woman pointed to a building on the horizon. Tall. Glass. The government center.

"That's where they're building it. The hub. The core. It's in the basement. Three levels down."

"That's insane."

"Yes. But it's true."

I looked at the building. At the woman. At the small device in my hand.

One person at a time.

Against an army.

"Hector," I said. "What do you think?"

"I think we're about to do something very stupid."

"Is that the best you can do?"

"That's the most honest thing I can say."

I started walking toward the building.

Behind me, the ghosts followed.

Above us, the sky was dark.

And somewhere inside that building, Threshold was waiting.

Ready to be born.
