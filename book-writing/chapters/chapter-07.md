# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 7: The Edge of Everywhere

The underground command center was cold.

Not just physically cold, though it was that too. The kind of cold that seeped into your bones and made you question everything you'd ever done to end up in a place like this.

Elena led us through the final corridor. She'd told me her name on the way down. Former NSA analyst. Twenty years digging through data until she'd found things she wasn't supposed to find. Then she'd disappeared before they could make her disappear.

"I was on their list," she'd explained. "The disappearances. I was supposed to be next. But I got out first."

Now she stood before a wall of screens, her fingers moving across a interface that looked like something out of a sci-fi movie.

"The map," she said. "Watch."

The holographic display flickered to life.

The city appeared below us. Beautiful, in a terrible way. All glowing lines and pulsing nodes.

Then the red started appearing.

Dots. Hundreds of them. Scattered across the map like a disease spreading.

"Each red dot is a disappearance," Elena said. "People taken in the last seventy-two hours. Anyone who's accessed restricted data. Anyone who's asked questions. Anyone who might know."

"How many?" I asked.

"Seventy-three. Just tonight."

The number hit me like a physical blow. Seventy-three people. Gone. Just like that.

"The government is denying it," Elena continued. "Calling it coincidence. Supply chain issues. Mass hysteria. But they're lying. And we have proof."

She pulled up another screen. Documents. Emails. Internal communications.

"Threshold was the plan," she said. "The implant network. Collect data. Build models. Predict behavior. But it failed. Too many variables. Too many errors. So they pivoted."

"Pivoted to what?"

"Plan B. Called it Contingency internally. Same basic concept, but different execution. Instead of predicting what you'll do, they just tell you what to do. Direct neural override. No choice. No free will. Just commands."

"That's insane."

"Yes. And it's already running."

A screen flickered. Sara's face appeared.

She was in a bunker somewhere. Fluorescent lights above. Bunk beds behind her. She looked tired. Scared. Alive.

"Connor!" She saw me on the screen. "Thank god. Listen to me. They've activated Contingency. It's not Threshold, it's worse. We have to—"

The signal died.

"Sara!" I shouted.

"Gone," Elena said. "They found the signal. We're compromised."

"How?"

"It doesn't matter. What matters is we're out of time."

The screens around us flickered. Warning lights began to pulse.

"Detecting incoming," Hector said. "Multiple hostiles. Three minutes out."

"Then we run."

We ran.

---

The tunnels beneath the city were a maze.

Elena led the way, her footsteps echoing off walls that hadn't seen light in decades. I followed, my lungs burning, my legs screaming for rest.

Behind us, the distant sound of boots. Soldiers. Coming closer.

"Here." Elena stopped at a door. Rusted. Forgot.

She pushed it open.

Beyond was an old subway station. Abandoned. Dusty. The kind of place where stories ended and new ones began.

We emerged into the night air. Cold. Sharp. Alive.

"Listen to me," Elena said. She pressed something into my hand.

It was small. Metallic. The size of a cigarette lighter.

"What is it?"

"A disruptor. Cuts the connection between implant and network. One person at a time."

I turned it over in my hand. Such a small thing. Against so much.

"And there are millions of them."

"Millions. Billions worldwide. We're not winning this with weapons, Connor. We win it by cutting the head off the snake."

She pointed.

In the distance, the government center rose against the sky. Glass and steel. Thirty-two floors of bureaucracy and control.

"That's where they're building it. The hub. The core. Three levels down. That's where Contingency lives."

"That's suicide."

"Probably." She smiled, but there was no humor in it. "But it's the only chance we have."

I looked at the building. At the device in my hand. At Elena.

"What if we fail?"

"Then we're all puppets anyway. Might as well go down fighting."

The night was quiet. Too quiet. The city hummed in the distance, oblivious to what was coming.

I started walking.

Toward the building.

Toward the core.

Toward whatever waited below.

Behind me, Elena followed.

Above us, the stars were hidden by clouds.

And somewhere in the darkness, Contingency was waiting.
