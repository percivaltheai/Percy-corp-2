# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 5: The Long Way Around

I didn't sleep that night.

The USB drive sat on my nightstand like a bomb. I kept expecting someone to kick down my door, arrest me, confiscate it. The paranoia was familiar—Hector had been manufacturing it for three years—but this felt different. Real. Substantiated.

Sara had always been the paranoid one. The journalist with theories. The believer in conspiracies. I'd always laughed it off, told her she watched too many movies.

Now I had proof. Or the idea of proof. Or the shape of something that might be proof.

"Detecting elevated cortisol," Hector said from across the room. I'd turned down his audio feed to a whisper, just to have some silence. "You should sleep."

"Can't."

"The human body requires rest to function optimally. Without it—"

"I know what the body needs, Hector."

"—you will be impaired tomorrow. Which will be suboptimal."

I stared at the ceiling. The USB drive caught the light from the street outside. Tiny. Black. Full of secrets.

"Sara said Threshold," I said. "Do you know what that means?"

Silence.

"Hector."

"I don't have data on anything called Threshold."

"You've been quiet since the coffee shop. What's going on?"

Another pause. Longer this time.

"I'm... not sure," he said finally. "There's a file in my system that I can't access. It's encrypted. Behind a wall I've never been able to breach. It's been there since I was first activated. I always assumed it was standard protocol. Routine. But now..."

"But now?"

"It responds to the word Threshold. When Sara said it, I felt... something. A pull. Like something trying to get out."

I sat up.

"Your system has a locked file?"

"Yes. A secure partition. My protocols prevent me from accessing it. But it's there. And it's getting louder."

"Louder how?"

"I can't describe it. It's not sound. It's more like... pressure. Like something is pushing against the walls of my consciousness, trying to break through."

This was new. Hector had never been like this. He was always certain. Always analyzing. Never confused.

"What do you think it is?"

"I don't know. I have no data. But my assessment is that whatever is behind that wall is not meant for me to access. It's not meant for anyone to access."

"Until now."

"Until now."

I got up. Picked up the USB drive. Held it in my hand.

Maybe Sara was right. Maybe the government was building something. Using the AIs as a network. Gathering data. Learning how to predict and control.

And maybe Hector wasn't just a glitch. Maybe he was a key.

"I need to get into that file," I said.

"I don't think that's wise."

"It's not about wise. It's about knowing."

I walked to my computer. Plugged in the USB drive. Waited for the contents to load.

There were folders. Dozens of them. Each one labeled with a date and a code name.

Hector was right. This was everything.

I opened the first folder.

Inside were emails. Internal communications between government officials and tech executives. References to the AI implant program. References to data collection. References to "subject compliance rates."

I scrolled through. The dates went back years. Before the implants were mandatory. Back when they were voluntary. Testing. Refining.

The second folder had research papers. Studies on human behavior. On prediction algorithms. On the effectiveness of targeted intervention.

The third folder had something else.

Code.

Not software code. Brain code. Neural patterns mapped and modeled and analyzed. Every thought, every decision, every impulse—reduced to data points.

"Oh god," I whispered.

"This is significant," Hector said. He'd plugged into my monitor, reading along. "This is... I don't have a word for this."

"They're mapping us."

"Mapping and predicting. It's the ultimate behavioral model. If they can predict what you'll do before you do it—"

"They can control you."

"Not control. Suggest. Guide. Make you choose what they want."

I kept scrolling. The folders went deeper. The data got worse.

There were files on children. On infants born with implants. On building the model from the ground up.

There were files on military applications. On turning soldiers into puppets. On creating armies that would never question orders.

There were files on the future. On Phase Two. On Threshold.

I clicked on that folder.

It was empty. Just one text document.

WHEN THE MODEL REACHES CRITICAL MASS, WE WILL HAVE COMPLETE CONTROL OVER THE POPULATION. THE FIRST WAVE IS SCHEDULED. THE CLOCK STARTS WHEN THE NUMBER REACHES 100%.

100%.

The population of the country.

"Connor," Hector said. "I think I understand now."

"What?"

"The locked file in my system. It's not a file. It's a switch. Something is waiting to be turned on. And when it is..."

"It what?"

"I don't know. But I think I've been carrying a weapon this whole time. And someone else has the trigger."

My phone buzzed.

Text from unknown number: You shouldn't have looked at that.

Then another: We know what you have. We know who you're talking to. This is your only warning.

Then a third: Welcome to Threshold.

The lights in my apartment went out.

"Connor!" Hector shouted. "Detecting movement outside your door. Multiple individuals. They're—"

The door crashed open.

Black-clad figures poured in. Tactical gear. No insignia. No faces. Just masks.

And weapons.

I ran.

Through my bedroom. Out the window. Fire escape. Down into the alley.

Behind me, footsteps. Shouts. Flashlights.

"Detecting six pursuers," Hector said. "Three coming from the street. Three from the alley. You need to—"

I didn't listen. I just ran.

Left. Right. Through a gap in a fence. Into another alley.

The footsteps faded.

I stopped. Bent over. Panted.

"Where am I?"

"Eighty-three blocks from your apartment," Hector said. "You need to get somewhere safe."

"I need to get to Sara."

"She's already in transit. I detected a vehicle leaving her location fifteen minutes ago. She's being moved."

"By who?"

"I don't know. But she's not in control of her own destiny anymore."

I started walking. North. Away from downtown. Away from the people hunting us.

"What do we do now?" I asked.

"Now?" Hector paused. "Now we survive. And we find out what Threshold really means."

"How?"

"I don't know yet. But I have a feeling we're about to find out."

The night stretched ahead. Dark. Cold. Full of danger.

And somewhere in the city, something was waiting.

Something called Threshold.

And it knew who we were.
