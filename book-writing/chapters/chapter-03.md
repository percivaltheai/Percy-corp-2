# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 3: Dead Air

My phone buzzed at 2:47 PM.

I didn't recognize the number. Should have let it go to voicemail. Should have.

"Hello?"

"Connor." A voice I hadn't heard in two years. Sara. My ex. The one who'd left when she realized I wasn't going to get rid of Hector. The one who'd said, I can't compete with a military AI for your attention.

Not in those words. But that was the gist.

"Sara?"

"Don't say my name." Her voice was tight. Strained. "Listen to me. I need to see you. In person. Tonight."

"What? Why?"

"I can't explain on this line. Just that thing you warned me about. The thing you said was wrong. I found something. I know why they—"

The line went dead.

I pulled the phone away from my ear. Looked at the screen.

Call ended.

"What the hell," I muttered.

"Unknown caller," Hector said. "Area code matches your previous romantic partner, Sara Chen. She appeared stressed. Heart rate elevated. I'm detecting deception in sixty-three percent of her statements."

"You can detect heart rate through a phone call?"

"I can detect the micro-tremors in her voice patterns that correlate with elevated stress. And she was definitely lying about something."

"About what?"

"I don't know. The call was interrupted before she could complete her false statement."

I stared at my phone. Sara and I had been together three years. She'd tolerated Hector for six months before she couldn't take it anymore. The tactical assessments of our dates. The security scans of her apartment. The time Hector had classified her mother as a "potential social engineering threat" because she'd asked when we were getting married.

That was the end. She'd left without ceremony, without explanation. Just a text: I can't do this anymore.

Two years of silence. And now this.

"She wants to meet," I said. "Tonight."

"Understood. I have identified seventeen possible meeting locations. Nine are acceptable. Eight are ambush sites."

"It's not an ambush."

"How can you be certain?"

"Because she's my ex. Ambushes aren't really her style."

"Ex-partners are statistically the most likely source of interpersonal conflict. This is well-documented."

I rubbed my eyes. The spreadsheet on my screen blurred. Meridian data, Derek's project, all of it suddenly felt irrelevant.

"She found something," I said. "That's what she said. Something about why they..."

"Why they what?"

"I don't know. Why they what, Hector?"

The question hung there. I didn't know what she meant. The thing I'd warned her about. The thing I'd said was wrong. What had I said?

I thought back. Two years ago. Before she'd left. We'd been arguing about the AIs, about Hector specifically. I'd said something. About the government. About the implants. About how something felt off.

What had I said?

"You told her the government was lying about the AI implementation," Hector said. "You said there was a reason they were pushing so hard for universal adoption. You said it wasn't about optimization. It was about control."

I remembered now. Late nights. Sara working on her journalism pieces. Me, cynical from years of corporate data analysis. We'd talked about conspiracy theories, laughed them off. But there'd been something in my gut. Something that didn't add up.

Why would the government give everyone a free AI? What were they really getting?

"Your physiological indicators suggest you're experiencing memory retrieval," Hector observed. "And possibly regret."

"Probably."

"I recommend we gather more intelligence before the meeting. I'll need to scan her current location, verify her identity, assess—"

"We're not doing any of that."

"Then we're operating blind. This is inadvisable."

"Inadvisable is my specialty."

I pulled up my phone. Texted the number: Where?

Three dots appeared. Then: Coffee shop. The one on Ninth. 8 PM. Come alone.

Alone. That was going to be a problem.

"She specifically requested you attend without me," Hector said. "This is suspicious."

"She's my ex. She doesn't want to meet the AI that's been assessing her as a threat for two years. That makes perfect sense."

"It makes her a liability risk."

"It makes her human."

I went back to my spreadsheet. Tried to focus. The numbers blurred. Sara's voice kept playing in my head. The fear in it. The urgency.

She'd found something. Something about why they were really doing this.

The government push for universal AI adoption. Mandatory implants. Everyone tracked, optimized, managed.

Was it really just about efficiency?

Or was there something else?

My phone buzzed again.

Text from Sara: Don't tell anyone. Especially not your AI.

"She knows I'm listening," Hector said. "This confirms she's a security risk."

"She knows you're annoying. That's different."

I spent the rest of the afternoon trying to work. Getting nothing done. Watching the clock. Waiting for eight o'clock.

At 6:30, I left early. Derek could wait. The Meridian account could wait. Nothing else mattered except this.

The coffee shop on Ninth was fifteen blocks away. I walked. Hector narrated the route, identified potential ambush sites, rated each passing vehicle for threat level.

None of it registered. I was thinking about Sara. About what she'd found. About what I'd said two years ago that had apparently set her on this path.

"Connor."

"Yeah?"

"I'm detecting an anomaly."

"What kind of anomaly?"

"Three blocks ahead. A vehicle has been following us for four minutes. Washington plates. Government registration. It's matching our pace."

"It's traffic, Hector."

"Traffic doesn't usually maintain a consistent four-car gap for four blocks. This is deliberate."

I kept walking. Didn't look back. Don't give them the satisfaction.

"The vehicle is turning," Hector said. "It's pulling into a side street. Wait—it's stopping. Someone is getting out."

I stopped.

"Don't turn around," Hector said. "But I'm now detecting two individuals on foot. They're approaching from behind. Estimated arrival: thirty seconds."

My heart rate spiked. Adrenaline, Hector would call it. The body's threat response.

"They're passing," he said. "Walking past you. Continuing south."

I exhaled.

"False alarm," I said. "Just people."

"Perhaps. Or perhaps they're establishing a pattern. Watching to see if you'll run."

"I didn't run."

"This time."

I kept walking. Faster now. The coffee shop was coming up. Ninth Street. The old brick building with the faded awning.

Sara was already there.

I could see her through the window. Sitting in the back corner. A coffee cup in front of her. She looked thinner than I remembered. Dark circles under her eyes. Hair pulled back in a tight bun.

She looked scared.

"Detecting elevated stress from your former partner," Hector said. "Seventy-eight percent probability she's planning something dangerous."

"Or she's just scared."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

I pushed through the door.

The coffee shop was nearly empty. Late evening, most people had gone home. A barista wiped down the counter. Someone typed on a laptop in the corner.

Sara looked up.

Our eyes met.

And for a moment, everything else fell away. Two years of silence. All the hurt. All the questions.

She'd found something.

She knew why.

That was enough.
