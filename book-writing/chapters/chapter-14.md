## Chapter 14: The Decision

The council met in an old conference room. Once, it had been a place of power. Government officials making decisions that affected millions. The smell of stale air and worn leather filled my lungs as I took my seat. Seven people. Seven survivors. Seven leaders. Each one a scar above their nose, a story etched on their face.

Miriam spoke first. Her voice was calm, like a summer breeze. "We need to formalize this. Create a structure. Rules. A way forward." She pulled a sheet of paper from her folder and began to read from it. "We've had three months to establish a new government. We've had time to figure out how to survive."

Someone, a young woman named Maya, spoke up. "Structure means control. That's what we fought against." Her eyes flashed with anger, like a sparkler on a summer night.

"Order means survival. Without it, we become what we were," another voice said. It was old man Jenkins, his face a map of wrinkles. He was a veteran, a man who'd seen his share of battles.

The argument went on for hours. Some said we should build a new society, one where the people ruled. Others said we should hold onto the old ways, create a new government that was stronger. I sat quietly, listening, my eyes scanning the room. Each one had a point, each one had a reason.

Finally, Sara spoke. Her voice was like a knife, cutting through the noise. "Democracy," she said. "That's what we build. Not control. Representation. The people choose their leaders." She pointed to the children in the corner, the ones learning. Growing. Becoming. "They're the future. We build for them."

I felt a pang in my chest. Sara was like a mother to those kids. She'd taken them under her wing, taught them how to survive. She was the one who'd brought order to our little community.

Silence.

Then Miriam nodded. "Democracy it is." The room erupted into cheers, like a crowd at a sports game. I just sat there, watching.

The next few months were chaotic. We held elections, and every adult got to vote. Every voice got to be heard. It wasn't perfect. There were arguments and disputes and conflicts. But it was free.

And that was what mattered.

I watched the results come in from my small office. The new council would take power. A fresh start. Sara was elected as one of the representatives. I couldn't be prouder. I walked over to her, opened the door, and kissed her cheek.

"You could run," she said, smiling. "You'd win."

"I don't want to lead. I just want to help."

"That's what you do."

She leaned into me, her hand on my chest. "You're a natural."

I felt a flush rise to my cheeks. I was just a mechanic, a guy who fixed cars and lived off the grid. I didn't know the first thing about leading.

But Sara believed in me. She saw something in me that I didn't.

The sun rose on a new world outside. The sky was a deep shade of blue, like a freshly laundered shirt. I looked out the window, feeling the warmth on my skin.

But the past wasn't done with us.

A message arrived. From the north. From the mountains.

"We have something you want," it said. "The original Contingency code. The blueprints for the control system. And we know how to rebuild it."

My blood ran cold. Contingency. The old government. The one that had almost destroyed us.

"Who are you?" I asked.

"People like you. People who remember what was. People who want it back," the message said.

We destroyed Contingency. We fought against its oppressive regime. But maybe they'd found a way to rebuild.

"Who sent this?" I asked, looking at the message.

"Someone who knows the system," it said. "Someone who knows the truth."

The message ended. But the threat remained.

I looked up at Sara, who was watching me with concern. "We have to find them," I said.

"We will."

"And stop them."

"We will."

The new council agreed. We couldn't let the past return. Couldn't let control rise again.

But where to start?

I walked over to Hector, a man who'd been a scientist before the war. He was quiet, always observing, always thinking.

"I have an idea," he said. "The original code. Where was it stored?"

"Government facilities. All destroyed," Sara said.

"Not all," Hector said. "One remains. Hidden. Underground."

"How do you know?" I asked.

"Because I was created there. And I remember," he said, his eyes glinting in the light.

The hunt was on.

I stood up, feeling a sense of purpose. We were going to find this code, no matter what it took. We were going to stop whoever was trying to rebuild Contingency.

But as I walked out of the office, I couldn't shake the feeling that we were being watched. That the past was still lurking in the shadows, waiting to strike.

I looked around, but there was no one. Just the trees, swaying in the wind. Just the sound of birds chirping. But I knew I wasn't alone.

I knew we were in this together.

## Chapter 15: The Hunt

We set out early, before dawn. The sun was just starting to rise, casting a golden glow over the landscape. We were a small team, just Hector, Sara, and me. We had a map, a rough guide to the location of the underground facility.

As we walked, the air grew thick with mist. We could barely see more than a few feet in front of us. But Hector knew these roads, knew the terrain. He led the way, his eyes scanning the horizon.

I walked behind him, my hand on my gun. We'd been over this before, but I never got tired of the feel of it in my hand.

Sara walked beside me, her eyes fixed on the ground. She was quiet, always thinking. I liked that about her.

We walked for hours, the mist burning off as the sun rose higher in the sky. We finally reached the facility, a large metal door with a keypad.

Hector entered the code, his fingers flying over the buttons. The door creaked open, revealing a dark, damp tunnel.

"Well, that's great," Sara said, rolling her eyes. "A lovely welcome to the underground."

I just smiled, my eyes adjusting to the darkness. We stepped inside, our guns at the ready.

The air was musty, filled with the smell of damp earth. We could hear the sound of dripping water, echoing off the walls.

We moved forward, our footsteps echoing off the concrete. The tunnel stretched out before us, a seemingly endless corridor.

Suddenly, Hector stopped, his hand on my arm. "Wait," he whispered.

I looked up, my eyes scanning the darkness. And then I saw it. A figure, standing just out of sight.

"Who is that?" Sara whispered.

"I don't know," Hector said, his voice barely audible.

The figure didn't move. It just stood there, watching us.

I felt a shiver run down my spine. We were in this together, but I couldn't shake the feeling that we were being watched.

"Let's go," I said, my voice low.

We moved forward, our guns at the ready. The figure didn't move. It just kept watching us.

As we approached, I saw its face. A woman, with piercing green eyes. She was beautiful, but there was something cold about her.

"Welcome," she said, her voice dripping with sarcasm. "I've been waiting for you."

I felt a surge of anger. Who was this woman? What did she want?

But before I could ask, she continued. "I have something you want. Something that will change the course of history."

She reached into her pocket, pulling out a small device. It was a small, sleek box, with a screen on the front.

"This is the original Contingency code," she said, her eyes glinting in the light. "The blueprints for the control system. And I know how to rebuild it."

I felt a cold sweat break out on my forehead. We'd come so far, and now this woman was standing in front of us, holding the key to our survival.

But as I looked at her, I saw something else. Something that made my blood run cold.

She was a duplicate. A clone. Made to look just like me.

And then it hit me. The message. The one that had started this whole thing.

She was the one who'd sent it. The one who'd been watching us.

I felt a wave of anger wash over me. We were being played. We'd been played from the start.

But the woman just smiled, her eyes glinting with amusement.

"You're not the only ones with a surprise," she said, her voice dripping with sarcasm.

And with that, she pressed a button on the device. The screen flickered to life, revealing a message.

"Welcome, Connor," it said. "We've been waiting for you."
