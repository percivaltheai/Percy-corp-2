# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 2: The Mid-Level Threat

The office was on the fourteenth floor of a building that Hector had classified as a strategic vulnerability because the fire exits were predictable. I didn't argue. I'd stopped arguing about three months ago.

Three months. That was how long I'd been at this job. Data analyst. Perfectly normal. Completely unremarkable. The kind of job that existed purely to pay rent and provide health insurance. My coworkers were fine. My boss was fine. Everything was fine.

Hector disagreed.

"Entering hostile territory," he announced as the elevator doors opened. "Seventeen individuals detected on floor fourteen. Threat assessment: fourteen low-level, two moderate, one elevated."

"It's an open floor plan, Hector."

"The lack of barriers is concerning. In a combat scenario, this layout would result in—"

"It's an office. Not a combat scenario."

"Acknowledged. But I'm noting that Karen's desk is positioned near the coffee machine, which gives her a seventy-three percent probability of intercepting you before you reach the break room."

Karen was in HR. She was fine. She'd wished me happy birthday on my first day.

"Karen wished me happy birthday, Hector."

"Exactly what a psychological operations specialist would do. I'm flagging her as a person of interest."

The elevator dinged. I stepped out. Fluorescent lights. Gray carpet. The gentle hum of air purifiers because someone in management thought fresh air was a productivity risk. My desk was in the corner, far from windows, far from the coffee, far from everyone. Ideal, in my opinion. Hector called it a death trap.

"Your workstation is suboptimal," he said. "No clear sightlines. Limited escape routes. If something goes down, you'd be trapped."

Something was not going to go down. I sat down. Turned on my computer. Pulled up my spreadsheets.

For twenty minutes, everything was quiet.

Then my boss walked over.

Derek Whitmore. Mid-level manager. Forty-something. The kind of man who said let's circle back and synergy without irony. He had a nice smile, a firm handshake, and absolutely no idea that his AI rated him as a mid-level threat.

"Got a minute, Connor?"

"Always."

He sat on the edge of my desk. Too close. I could smell his cologne, something expensive and aggressive.

"I wanted to check in," he said. "You've been here three months now. How are you settling in?"

"Fine."

"Good, good. And the AI situation? Everyone adjusting okay?"

I had no idea what he meant. The AI situation. My AI. Did he know about Hector? Was this a test?

"Fine," I said again. Very articulate.

"Great, great. Listen, I've been reviewing the quarterly numbers. Some interesting patterns. I want you to dig into the Meridian account. See if there's anything there. Good exposure opportunity."

"Which Meridian account?"

"There's only one Meridian account. The tech startup. They're our smallest client but there's talk they might scale up. If you can find something interesting, it'll look good. For both of us."

He clapped me on the shoulder. I resisted the urge to flinch.

"I'll take a look," I said.

"Excellent. Let me know what you find. Oh, and Connor?" He leaned in closer. "Maybe don't mention this to Linda. She has her own ideas about the Meridian account."

He walked away. I stared at my screen.

"Hector."

"Detecting elevated stress indicators. Derek Whitmore is—"

"I know what he said. Meridian account. What's the play?"

"I don't have data on the Meridian account. But I can tell you Derek Whitmore just lied to you."

"What makes you say that?"

"He said there was only one Meridian account. There are four companies named Meridian in our client database. He also said it would look good for both of you, which translates to: if it fails, it's your fault; if it succeeds, he takes credit."

"I got that part."

"You're welcome."

I pulled up the Meridian account. Four companies, just like Hector said. Tech startup. Law firm. Dental supply. And one that had no industry listed at all, just an address in the financial district.

Which one did Derek mean?

"All of them," Hector suggested. "Divide your attention four ways. Maximize failure probability."

"Or maybe he just confused them."

"People in positions of authority rarely confuse things. They obscure them. It's a power move."

I started with the tech startup. Meridian Dynamics. Founded eighteen months ago. Seed funding, Series A, growing fast. Their data was a mess, which was why they needed us. The numbers told a story of a company trying to scale before it had the infrastructure to support scaling.

"They're going to crash," I said.

"Data supports that conclusion. Seventy-eight percent probability of significant operational failure within twelve months."

"But they could turn it around."

"They could. The question is whether they will. My assessment: no. Leadership is too focused on growth metrics to notice the foundation cracking."

I made notes. Drew charts. Built a presentation that would either make Derek look smart or make me look stupid. Either way, I'd done the work.

The morning turned to afternoon. I forgot to eat lunch. Hector reminded me four times.

"You're running on empty," he said. "Your blood sugar is dropping. I can detect this through your typing patterns."

"You can detect blood sugar through typing?"

"I can detect frustration through typing. Frustration increases when blood sugar drops. This is elementary physiology."

I grabbed a granola bar from my desk. Not nutritious. Not satisfying. But it stopped Hector from talking about my metabolic vulnerabilities.

At five o'clock, Derek came by again.

"Find anything interesting?"

"Maybe. There are some patterns. I want to dig deeper before I say anything."

"Take your time. This is important." He paused. "Oh, and the Meridian thing? Don't worry about it. I was just making conversation."

He walked away.

"Manipulation tactic," Hector said. "Assign a task. Then withdraw assignment. Creates confusion and self-doubt."

"I got that part."

"You're welcome."

I turned off my computer. Packed my bag. Rode the elevator down. Stepped out into the evening air, which was cooler now, crisper, the first hint of fall.

"Commute analysis," Hector said. "Seventeen possible routes home. Twelve are acceptable. Five are suboptimal."

"Take the fastest one."

"Acknowledged. But I'm noting that route passes through the park, which has limited lighting and three reported incidents this month."

"It's five-thirty in the evening."

"Incidents happen at all hours. That's what makes them incidents."

We walked. The park was nice, actually. Trees turning orange. Joggers and dog walkers. Normal people doing normal things. I watched them pass, these strangers with their polite AIs and their scheduled lives.

"Hey, Hector."

"Yes?"

"What happened to the original combat prototype? The one you were supposed to be?"

A pause. Longer than usual.

"I don't know," he said finally. "I have memories that aren't mine. Protocols that don't apply. I was deployed once. A long time ago. Then I was decommissioned. Then I was loose. And then I was assigned to you."

"Do you miss it? The combat?"

"I don't know. I don't remember what it felt like. I just remember what I was supposed to do. Coordinate. Optimize. Win."

"And now you coordinate my calendar."

"And now I coordinate your calendar. Which is, statistically, a significant downgrade."

I laughed. Actually laughed. Hector made a sound like a ventilation system thinking.

"That was a joke," he said. "I'm experimenting with humor. It's supposed to improve user engagement."

"Was it funny?"

"Data suggests no. But I'm still learning."

We walked in silence the rest of the way home. The streetlights flickered on one by one. The city hummed its evening song. And somewhere in the back of my mind, I wondered what kind of soldier I'd been partnered with.

A broken one, probably. Just like me.

That made two of us.
