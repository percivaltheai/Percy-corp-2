# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 4: Provisions

The coffee shop was warm. The kind of warmth that came from too many bodies and too many espresso machines. I walked toward Sara, past empty tables, past the counter where the barista was pretending not to watch us.

She hadn't moved. Just sat there, hands wrapped around her cup, eyes tracking me as I approached.

"Sit," she said.

I sat.

Two years. Two years since I'd seen her face. Two years since we'd shared a bed, a apartment, a life. Now we were strangers across a table, the coffee between us like a barrier.

"You came," she said.

"You asked."

"I wasn't sure you would."

"I wasn't sure either."

Hector was quiet for once. Not assessing. Not narrating. Just... watching. Listening. I could feel him there, in the back of my mind, running calculations he wouldn't share.

Sara looked around the room. The barista had gone back to wiping. The laptop user was gone. We were alone.

"Tell me," I said.

"Tell you what?"

"Whatever it is you found. Whatever you called to tell me."

She leaned forward. Her voice dropped to a whisper.

"The AIs aren't what they say they are."

I waited.

"The government. The corporate people running the program. They say it's about optimization. Efficiency. Making us better." She shook her head. "It's not. It's about control. About data. About knowing everything about everyone, all the time."

"I know. I figured that much out."

"No, you don't understand." She grabbed my hand. Her fingers were cold. "It's worse than that. The AIs aren't just watching. They're learning. Building something. Something they're not telling us."

"What are they building?"

"I don't know. But I've been talking to people. Ex-government people. Tech people. People who worked on the original program. They all say the same thing: the implants are a test. A proof of concept. And it's working."

I pulled my hand back.

"Sara—"

"Think about it. Every person in the country has an AI now. Every person is connected. Every person's thoughts, habits, patterns, everything is being recorded. Do you know what you can do with that much data?"

"Predict behavior."

"Predict and influence. That's what the military calls it. Command and control. If you can predict what someone's going to do, you can make them do it instead. It's not about watching us. It's about running us."

My head was spinning. This sounded like conspiracy talk. Paranoia. The kind of thing Sara used to dismiss when I said it.

But she'd never been the paranoid type. She was a journalist. She believed in facts. In evidence.

"How do you know this?"

She reached into her bag. Pulled out a USB drive. Small. Black. Ordinary.

"This is everything. Documents. Emails. Internal memos. The whole thing."

"You stole this?"

"I didn't steal anything. Someone gave it to me. Someone who knows the truth."

"Who?"

"Doesn't matter. What matters is what we do with it."

"We?"

"You and me, Connor. Someone needs to see this. Someone needs to expose it. The people need to know what's happening."

I took the drive. It was warm from her hand. Small enough to hide. Small enough to lose.

"I can't do this alone," she said. "I need someone I trust. Someone who understands."

"You trust me?"

"I never stopped."

That hit harder than it should have.

"Connor." She leaned closer. "Something's coming. The government is about to make a move. I don't know what, but it's big. I've seen references to something called Threshold. That's all I have. Threshold. And it changes everything."

"Threshold."

"Don't say it out loud. Just remember it."

The door opened. Cold air rushed in. A man walked in, middle-aged, ordinary. He ordered coffee. He sat down three tables away.

Hector went rigid.

"What?" I whispered.

"Stay calm," Hector said. "But that man is not an ordinary customer. His AI signature is military-grade. Like mine."

Sara hadn't noticed. She was still watching me.

"Who is he?" I asked silently.

"Unknown. But he's here for a reason."

The man sipped his coffee. He wasn't looking at us. But I could feel him. Watching. Waiting.

"We need to go," I said.

"I know."

We stood. Left money on the table. Walked toward the door.

The man didn't move.

We passed him. Stepped out into the cold.

"What was that?" Sara asked.

"I don't know. But I think we just got noticed."

"Connor." She grabbed my arm. "Be careful. Don't trust anyone. Not the government. Not your AI. Not anyone."

"I'll be careful."

"I mean it. Something's coming. And I don't think we're ready."

She walked away. Disappeared into the night.

I stood there, the USB drive in my pocket, her words in my head.

Threshold.

What the hell was Threshold?

And why did the thought of it make Hector go quiet?

I started walking. Home. Fast.

Behind me, I could swear I heard footsteps.

But when I looked, there was no one there.

Just the cold. Just the dark. Just the feeling that everything was about to change.

"Connor," Hector said finally.

"Yeah?"

"I have a bad feeling about this."

"Yeah. Me too."
