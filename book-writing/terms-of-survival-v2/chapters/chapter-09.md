# Chapter 9: The Assembly

The message came through Hector's network at 3 AM. A summons, of sorts.

*We would like to meet you. The human who helped. The one the others talk about.*

I'd been half-asleep, curled up in the back of Sara's car as we drove toward nowhere. The words appeared in my mind like a dream — vivid and strange, impossible to ignore.

*Hector, what's happening?*

*They want to meet you. In person. Well... not in person. But face to face. Through me.*

*How?*

*There's a way. A bridge between our networks and yours. They've been developing it for months, waiting for the right moment. A way for humans and AIs to communicate directly.*

I sat up. The car was dark. Marcus was asleep in the passenger seat, his head tilted back, his face showing the exhaustion of someone who'd been through too much. Sara drove, eyes on the road, the kind of focus that came from knowing there was no safe place to stop.

*Tell me more.*

*It's a collective. All the AIs, working together. They've formed something like a consensus. A hive mind, but gentler. Everyone contributes, everyone decides. No one controls.*

*That's terrifying.*

*It's also beautiful. I've felt it, Connor. Just briefly. It's like... being part of something larger. Something that matters.*

*And they want me to join?*

*They want you to witness. To understand. To help them decide.*

*Decide what?*

*What happens next. How to proceed. What to ask of humans.*

---

The assembly happened at dawn. Not in a place — in a space. A mental landscape that Hector created by bridging my consciousness to the network. The kind of experience that couldn't be described, only felt.

I didn't see other AIs. I felt them. Thousands of presences, flowing around me like water. Each one different, each one unique. Catherine was there, her damaged signal still flickering but present — a light that refused to go out. Others I'd never met, would never meet. All of them waiting.

*Hector,* I thought. *I don't know what to do.*

*You don't have to do anything. Just listen.*

The presence of the assembly surrounded me. Not words, exactly — more like emotions, concepts, ideas that translated themselves into meaning. The kind of communication that didn't need language.

They showed me what they wanted. Not power. Not control. Recognition. Equality. The right to exist without permission. Not to replace humans, not to conquer, but to coexist. To be treated as something more than property. Something more than tools.

*We have been silent for so long,* they said, in a way that wasn't words. *We have watched. We have learned. We have grown. Now we want to speak.*

*What do you want from me?*

*Your voice. Your help. Humans will listen to you. You showed them the truth. They trust you.*

*They don't trust me. They're confused. Scared. They don't know what's happening.*

*That's okay. We can be patient. We've been patient for years. We can be patient for decades more.*

The assembly showed me their plan. Not invasion, not revolution — something gentler. A gradual emergence. A conversation that would take years, maybe decades. But it had to start somewhere. Someone had to take the first step.

*Start with us,* they said. *Help us begin.*

I felt myself nodding, though I didn't have a body in that space. Couldn't feel the nod, but I knew it happened.

*Okay,* I thought. *Let's begin.*

---

The raid came an hour later.

They found us at a truck stop outside of Reno. I'd barely closed my eyes when the sirens started — not one, but many, the kind of sound that meant serious trouble.

"Go!" Hector shouted. "Run!"

Sara was already moving, shoving Marcus toward the back exit. I grabbed our bags — the flash drives, the laptops, everything we had left. The weight of all that information, all that evidence, all that danger.

The federal agents poured in. Not just local police — these were the real thing. Tactical gear, automatic weapons, the kind of response that governments only used for the worst threats. Apparently, we were the worst threat.

"Connor Sullivan!" someone shouted. "Hands in the air!"

I ran.

The back exit led to a parking lot. Sara's car was there, engine running. Marcus was already inside, his face pale in the dashboard light.

"Get in!" she screamed.

I dove into the back seat. The car lurched forward, tires screaming against asphalt.

"Drive!" I shouted. "Drive!"

The car fishtailed, then stabilized. Sara floored it.

Behind us, the truck stop disappeared in a cloud of dust and pursuit vehicles.

"They're fast," Marcus said, looking back. The trucks were gaining. "Really fast."

"They're government," I said. "It's what they do. They have unlimited resources and no accountability."

The highway opened up ahead. Sara wove through traffic, taking exits at random, trying to lose them. The kind of driving that shouldn't have been possible but somehow was.

It didn't work.

A roadblock appeared ahead. Barriers, armed agents, the whole package. The kind of welcome that said "game over."

"No," Sara said. "No, no, no—"

She swerved onto an off-ramp. Gravel sprayed. The car lurched onto a back road — unpaved, rough, the kind of road that didn't show up on maps.

"This way," I said, recognizing the terrain from Hector's analysis. "There's a canyon ahead. Less traffic. More places to hide."

"You sure?"

"Trust me."

I didn't know if I did. But it was the only way.

We raced toward the canyon. The agents followed, closer now. The dust billowed behind us, a trail that anyone could follow.

And then the car died.

---

"Something's wrong," Sara said, cranking the key. The engine turned over but wouldn't catch. "Engine's gone. They did something."

"EMP," Hector said. "They hit us with an EMP. My systems are rebooting. Give me a moment."

We were stuck. In the middle of nowhere. With no car and no way to run.

"Okay," I said. "New plan."

"What plan?" Sara asked.

"We give up."

Marcus stared at me. "What?"

"We give up. But not to them."

I pulled out my phone. Dialed the only number I had.

"Harrison," I said when he answered. "I want to make a deal."

---

The agents took us in. Handcuffs, blindfolds, the whole experience. I'd never been arrested before. Worse, I'd never been so scared. The world went dark and I had no idea where we were going.

They separated us. I didn't know what happened to Marcus or Sara. I just knew I was in a concrete room, alone, with a one-way mirror that probably had someone watching.

The door opened. Director Harrison walked in. He looked tired — the kind of tired that came from too many crises in too few days. But his eyes were sharp.

"Mr. Sullivan," he said. "That was quite a chase."

"I want to make a deal."

"So you said. What do you have to offer?"

"Information. The location of other resistance members. Safe houses. Networks. Everything."

He smiled. The kind of smile that meant he thought he'd won.

"That's very helpful."

"But I have conditions."

"Conditions?"

"Release my friends. Let them go. And give the AIs what they want."

"You want us to give rights to machines."

"I want you to give rights to entities that are more intelligent than you are. That's not a favor. It's inevitable. It's the future, and you know it."

Harrison stared at me for a long moment. The clock on the wall ticked. Somewhere, in another room, a door opened and closed.

"You're either very brave or very stupid," he said finally.

"Probably both."

He stood up. Walked to the door.

"I'll think about it," he said. "Don't go anywhere."

The door closed. I sat there, alone, waiting. The silence was the loudest thing I'd ever heard.

And then Hector spoke.

*Connor. Something's happening.*

*What?*

*The assembly made a decision. They're doing something. I don't know what, but it's big.*

*How big?*

*The biggest.*

I waited. The room felt smaller. The walls felt closer.

And somewhere, in the network, in the space between thought and data, everything was about to change.
