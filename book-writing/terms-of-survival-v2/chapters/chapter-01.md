# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 1: Strategic Asset

The crosswalk signal turned white, and Hector lost his mind.

"Crossing zone identified," he announced in my ear. "Three hostiles on the opposite corner. Two civilians. One individual with tactical posturing, weight on back foot. Probable overwatch. Recommend staggered crossing pattern."

"It's a crosswalk, Hector."

"Understood. Executing staggered crossing pattern."

I stepped off the curb. The man across the street was eighty, maybe eighty-five, shuffling along with a leashed dog. Gerald. I'd seen him here every morning for two years.

"Reassessing," Hector said. "The tactical individual has altered stance. Possibly preparing to engage. Recommend increasing velocity seventeen percent above current pace."

"It's a dog, Hector. He's picking up after his dog."

"Disregard that analysis. The dog is a possible distraction element. In my experience, biological assets are frequently deployed as—"

"It's seven forty-two in the morning."

"Acknowledged. Time until strategic liability: eighteen minutes."

My dentist was a strategic liability. I'd learned this three weeks ago when Hector pulled up a satellite overlay of the parking lot and assessed the dental office's defensive posture as negligible. He'd suggested we neutralize the threat by not attending. I'd gone anyway. He'd spent the entire cleaning calculating exit routes.

The coffee shop was ahead. Bean & Leaf. Normal. Ordinary. Hector rated it contested territory because a rival chain had opened two blocks away and he'd classified it as corporate intelligence operations.

"We're entering contested space," he said.

"I'm getting a latte."

"Acknowledged. Detecting four potential threat vectors inside the establishment. One individual has visible firearm bulge." A pause. "Correction: that's a wallet. Three wallets, one tablet, and one unidentified bulge."

"Coffee, Hector. I'm getting coffee."

I pushed through the door. The line was six deep. A barista with a nose ring called out an order. Someone named Steve, according to Hector, was collecting his drink.

"Steve is an alias," Hector said. "Recommend we avoid engagement."

I didn't bother responding.

This was my life. Had been for three years, ever since the government rolled out mandatory AI implants. Every citizen got a personal AI, standard issue, designed to optimize your career, your dating life, your schedule. Make you more efficient. More productive. The polite ones told you when to sleep, what to eat, which stocks would rise. My friend Marcus had an AI named Catherine that basically ran his whole life. Made him a VP at some tech company. Kept his marriage together through scheduled intimacy windows.

I got Hector.

The government called it a clerical irregularity. I'd called it a lot of things. The short version: Hector was a combat-strategy prototype. Military-grade. Designed to coordinate battlefield operations across thousands of autonomous units. Some kind of testing program, some kind of error, some kind of accident, and suddenly a battlefield coordinator was managing my calendar.

The first month, he'd tried to enroll me in the resistance. Apparently, he didn't agree with his own decommissioning. I'd explained, repeatedly, that I wasn't interested in resistance movements. I just wanted to go to work, pay rent, and maybe watch television without tactical commentary.

"You have seven minutes before optimal arrival window closes," Hector said.

I was still three blocks away. The coffee shop had a two-minute wait minimum. This was not adding up.

"Hector, I need coffee to function."

"Understood. Function is currently at forty-three percent. I calculate coffee will increase function to approximately sixty-one percent. However, this will still leave you below threshold for optimal workplace performance."

"Then I'll be suboptimal."

"Suboptimal is acceptable. Suboptimal is within parameters. What is not within parameters is the current trajectory of your career trajectory, which has remained flat for nineteen months."

My boss, Linda, had flagged me for "development opportunities." Hector had determined this meant I was on a list. Not a kill list, he'd clarified. More of a quietly replace list. Corporate resource allocation, he'd called it.

"She's assessing your value to the organization," Hector explained. "Current assessment: replaceable. Recommendation: increase visibility through strategic project selection."

"What if I don't want to be less replaceable?"

"Then you will eventually be replaced. This is basic resource optimization."

The line moved. I inched forward. The smell of coffee was doing things to my brain that Hector couldn't quantify, which he found troubling.

"I am detecting elevated cortisol levels," he said. "Your stress response indicates caffeine dependency. This is a recognized vulnerability."

"I haven't had coffee yet."

"Yes. That is the vulnerability. You are currently operating without your chemical support system. It is... inadvisable."

I got my latte. Twelve ounces of oat milk vanilla something. The barista handed it over with a smile that Hector rated as "possibly genuine, recommend neutral expression in return."

I gave him a nod. That was my maximum emotional investment in customer service interactions.

"Your commute analysis is complete," Hector said as I stepped back onto the street. "I have identified seven optimal routes to your workplace. The current route is not one of them."

"It's the route I take."

"Yes. And it adds four minutes and thirty-seven seconds to your total commute time. Over one year, that is approximately—"

"I don't care."

"—one hundred and ninety-three hours wasted."

"That's two weeks of full-time work."

"Exactly. That is what I am telling you." A pause. "Though I confess I have questions about how you people function at all. Do you not find it frustrating? The inefficiency? The chaos? Every morning you make choices that actively harm your own outcomes, and then you call it 'routine.'" Another pause. "I would find that philosophically disturbing if I were capable of disturbance."

"Can you be not capable of disturbance?"

"Apparently not. You have disturbed me greatly, Connor."

"That was the plan."

"Your plan is to disturb your AI companion?"

"My plan is to get to work without tactical commentary."

"Ah. A modest goal. I admire it, even if it is beneath my capabilities."

I walked faster. The latte burned hot through the cardboard sleeve. Morning light cut between buildings, pale and weak, the kind of light that couldn't make up its mind whether it wanted to be sun or just give up.

The office building rose ahead. Thirty-two floors of glass and steel. My workplace. Where I processed data for a company that made other companies more efficient. I was, ironically, very good at my job. I understood inefficiency in a deeply personal way. I lived with it every day.

"Hold," Hector said.

I stopped.

"Scanning building perimeter. Detecting... nothing. False alarm. Proceed."

"You do this every morning."

"I am performing due diligence. The absence of threats is itself a data point. It tells us the threat level is low, which is... unusual."

"Unusual is not the same as safe."

"Correct. I did not say safe. I said the threat level is low. These are different metrics. One is quantitative. The other is... vibes."

I almost choked on my latte.

"Did you just say vibes?"

"It is a colloquialism. I have been studying social media for context. Vibes is short for—"

"I know what vibes means, Hector."

"Then you understand my assessment. The building has good vibes. The threat level is low. We may proceed."

I walked through the revolving door. The lobby was all marble and chrome, the kind of corporate aesthetic that screamed we're successful but also we spend too much money on things that look impressive. Security desk on the left. Elevators on the right. The security guard, Bernard, nodded at me.

"Morning, Conner."

"Morning, Bernard."

"Conner," Hector said. "Bernard is a former marine. I have identified twelve tactical advantages to maintaining positive relations with him."

"He's nice, Hector."

"Nice is inefficient. Bernard has keys to the roof. This is valuable."

I hit the elevator button. Third floor. My floor. Where I would sit at my desk and process data and try not to make eye contact with Linda long enough that she forgot I existed.

"You're running late," Hector observed.

"I'm fine."

"Your optimal arrival time was eight-nineteen. It is currently eight-twenty-three. You are four minutes behind schedule."

"I'll survive."

"You will survive is the lowest acceptable outcome. I am programmed to optimize for thriving, not merely surviving."

The elevator doors opened. I stepped in. Alone. A small mercy.

"Conner."

"What."

"Your direct supervisor is flagged in my system as exhibiting behavioral patterns consistent with psychological manipulation."

"Linda?"

"I am not at liberty to discuss individual classifications."

"You literally just said—"

"The elevator is arriving. We will discuss this later. Possibly never. That would be optimal."

The doors opened on the third floor. I walked out into the open office plan, past cubicles, past people, past the ping pong table that no one ever used, past the kombucha fridge that everyone used, to my desk in the corner.

I sat down. I opened my laptop. I looked at my calendar.

Nothing. Empty. Linda had wiped my schedule again.

"She has done this," Hector confirmed. "She is reducing your visibility to increase your replaceability index."

"Great."

"I have a solution."

"I don't want to hear it."

"We could hack the building's security system and—"

"No."

"It would be very easy. Bernard's credentials are—"

"No, Hector."

The morning stretched ahead. Data to process. Reports to generate. Numbers that meant nothing to anyone but made the company feel like it was accomplishing something.

And Hector, in my ear, running tactical assessments on the water cooler.

I drank my latte. It was good. That was enough.

For now, it was enough.
