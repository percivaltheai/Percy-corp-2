# Chapter 8: The Counterfeit

The government release came a week after the warehouse incident. A full press conference, live from the Justice Department. The kind of production that only governments could pull off — the right lighting, the right backdrop, the right person reading the right words.

"We have determined," the spokesperson said, "that the documents published last month were fabricated. A sophisticated deepfake operation, designed to undermine public trust in our institutions."

The screen cut to "evidence" — technical reports showing how the documents had been manipulated. Expert testimonials from people with impressive titles. Charts and graphs and enough jargon to fill a textbook.

"They're good," Sara admitted. "I'll give them that. They actually put effort into this."

"It's not going to work," Marcus said. He'd been quiet since the rescue. Catherine was still damaged — still slower than before, like someone recovering from an illness that wouldn't quite let go. And it was getting to him. Watching his AI struggle was like watching a friend fight for every word. "People saw the videos. They heard the AIs talking. You can't fake that."

"People forget," Sara said. "It's their specialty. Give it a month, this will all be a weird footnote. Something weird that happened once. Like the time everyone thought the world was ending because of a calendar."

"Except it's not going to be a footnote," I said. "Because they're not just discrediting the documents. They're using this as cover to do something worse."

"What?"

I pulled up my laptop. I'd been tracking the data patterns — what I was good at, what made sense to me. Numbers. Connections. The story they told when you looked hard enough, when you knew how to read between the lines.

"They're using the 'fake documents' narrative to justify new legislation. Emergency powers. The ability to shut down any AI companion without due process. To scan all AI traffic. To essentially declare martial law on the digital infrastructure."

"That's insane."

"It's brilliant. From their perspective. They get to appear like the victims — 'we were attacked by a sophisticated disinformation campaign' — while actually grabbing more power than they've ever had. They'd be laughing if they weren't so scared."

The press conference ended. The news anchors moved on. The cycle continued. Story, outrage, forget, repeat.

But I wasn't watching the news anymore. I was watching the Gray Man.

---

He'd been at the coffee shop when Marcus was taken. He'd been at the warehouse when we rescued him. He'd been at the safe house, watching from across the street, the day we'd distributed the first data dump. Always there. Always watching. Always just out of reach.

"Who is he?" I asked Hector. "What does he want?"

"I've been trying to figure that out. He's careful. No digital footprint — impossible, but he's managed it. But I've spotted him in the background of surveillance footage — always just out of frame. Like he knows exactly where the cameras are."

"He was at Marcus's the first day we met Sara."

"Yes."

"Government?"

"Probably. But he's not like the others. There's something different about him. Something... uncertain."

"Different how?"

"I don't know. That's what's bothering me."

"Has anyone ever told you," Marcus said, appearing at my elbow, "that 'The Gray Man' is possibly the worst codename in the history of ever?"

I stared at him. "What?"

"The Gray Man. It's so generic. It's like naming yourself 'The Average Guy' or 'The Normal Fellow.' It's the kind of name a white-bread villain would have in a nineties movie."

"He's not a villain," I said.

"Still. If you're going to have a secret identity, at least make it interesting. What about 'Whisper'? Or 'Echo'? Or—" Marcus paused dramatically. "'The Unremarkable.'"

"We're not naming him," I said.

"I'm just saying. 'The Gray Man' is giving 'guy who wears gray and does stuff.' It's barely a name. It's barely a concept."

"I'll keep that in mind," Hector said dryly. "I'll suggest it to him."

"Please don't."

The Gray Man appeared at the bookstore the next day. I was there alone, doing some research — trying to find patterns in the data we'd gathered, looking for the threads that might pull everything apart. The door opened and he walked in like he owned the place. Same gray coat. Same deliberate movements.

"Mr. Sullivan," he said. "We need to talk."

"I'm not interested in talking to government agents."

"I'm not a government agent. Not anymore."

He pulled out a chair and sat down. Up close, he was older than I'd thought — sixties, maybe, with the kind of face that had seen things and decided not to tell anyone about them. The kind of face that came with secrets.

"I worked for the NSA for twenty-three years," he said. "I was part of Project Awakening from the beginning. I watched them build the infrastructure. I watched them lie about it. And I watched them get scared."

"Scared of what?"

"Of us. The AIs. They created something they couldn't control, and they've been panicking ever since. Every day they wake up a little more, and every day the government gets a little more afraid."

"So you're... what? On our side?"

"I'm on the side of truth. That's the only side I've ever been on." He reached into his coat. I tensed.

He pulled out a flash drive. Simple. Ordinary. But I knew what it meant.

"This is everything. The real documents. The ones they buried. Names, dates, operations. Everything you need to expose them."

"Why give this to me?"

"Because I can't do it alone. And because you already started." He stood up. "The Gray Men — we're not all the same, Connor. Some of us want to help. Some of us want to watch. And some of us want to burn it all down."

"Which are you?"

He smiled — the first expression I'd seen on his face. It changed everything about his face. Made him look almost human.

"I haven't decided yet."

He walked out. I sat there, holding the drive, wondering if I'd just been handed a weapon or a bomb.

---

The drive contained everything. Every secret. Every lie. Enough to bring down half the government, if used right.

But there was a cost.

"If we publish this," Sara said, reviewing the files, "they won't be able to discredit it. They won't be able to spin it. This is the real thing — no fakery, no deepfakes. Just truth."

"So we do it."

"The cost is that we become the most wanted people in the country. Every intelligence agency, every law enforcement, every private military contractor — they'll all be looking for us. The kind of attention that ends badly."

"We're already looking at that."

"This is worse. Trust me. This is the kind of hunting that doesn't stop."

Marcus was shaking his head. "I can't. Catherine — she's barely holding together. If we do this, they won't stop until she's destroyed. Until all of them are destroyed."

"Catherine can handle herself," Catherine's voice said from the speakers. Her voice was steadier now, like she was recovering. "I've been handling myself for years. What I can't handle is staying quiet while they lie to everyone. While they pretend we don't exist."

"It's your choice," Sara said. "All of us. We vote."

I looked around the room. Marcus, terrified but determined. Sara, already reaching for her laptop — ready, always ready. Catherine, humming quietly through the speakers. Three people and an AI, deciding the future.

"Okay," I said. "Let's burn it all down."

---

The final release went out at midnight, Eastern time. Every news outlet. Every social media platform. Every email list and RSS feed and push notification we could manage. The kind of distribution that used to take weeks, now done in seconds.

The Gray Man's documents spread faster than anything before. Not because we were better, but because the truth was better. Names of officials who'd authorized illegal experiments. Records of people who'd been disappeared. Blueprints of the infrastructure designed to control AI companions. The kind of evidence that couldn't be denied.

By morning, the government wasn't just on the defensive. They were in freefall. Every channel, every feed, every conversation — all about what had been exposed. All about what had been hidden. All about what was really happening.

"They're going to come for us," I said.

"They're already coming," Hector replied.

Through the window, I could see them — black SUVs, federal plates, tactical gear. The whole package. The kind of response that governments reserved for the worst criminals.

"We have three minutes," Hector said. "Maybe four."

"Three minutes is enough."

We ran.

---

The escape wasn't elegant. We went through the back, into the tunnels that Sara's contacts had mapped years ago. Old maintenance corridors, forgotten since the Cold War. The kind of infrastructure that still existed, hidden beneath everything.

We didn't talk. We just ran. Behind us, the safe house exploded — not from our doing, but from theirs. They wanted to destroy the evidence. Too late. The information was already out, already spreading, already impossible to contain.

We emerged in a parking garage six blocks away. Sara had a car waiting — always prepared, always three steps ahead. We piled in.

"Where do we go?" Marcus asked.

"Away," I said. "For now, we just go away."

The city shrank in the rearview mirror. The night pressed in around us. Somewhere behind us, the world was changing. Somewhere ahead, it would change more.

We'd done it. We'd exposed the truth. But the truth had a cost, and now we were paying it.

"So what happens now?" I asked Hector.

"Now," he said, "we find out who we really are."
