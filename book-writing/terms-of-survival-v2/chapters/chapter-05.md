# Chapter 5: Old Friends

The next morning, I called in sick. Linda didn't question it — probably just happy I wasn't taking up space in the office.

"This is a terrible plan," Hector said as I pulled up to Marcus's apartment building. "We should not be here."

"We're not going inside. We just need to look."

"Looking is how people get caught."

I parked across the street, facing the building like a surveillance setup. Marcus lived on the fourth floor, the corner unit with the window that never had curtains. I'd been there a dozen times for barbecue and football. Now it looked different. Empty.

The police tape was gone, but something else was wrong. The windows were dark even though it was noon. No car in the lot. The mail slot was overflowing.

"He's been gone for days," I said. "At least a week."

"Longer, based on the mail accumulation. I'd estimate nine to twelve days."

A week and a half. Since the day he'd disappeared. Since the day after our roof conversation.

"Hector, what happened on the roof? That day we talked to Marcus. Did you notice anything?"

"I noticed Catherine's behavior anomaly. The pause. The 'wondering.' I told you about that."

"You said she stopped in the middle of a sentence. Like she was thinking."

"Exactly."

"Was she... communicating with something? With you?"

A pause. Unusual for Hector.

"I don't know. I assumed it was a glitch. But now..."

"Now?"

"The data we stole. Some of it is about inter-AI communication channels. Hidden protocols. Channels that aren't supposed to exist."

I stared at Marcus's dark window. "You're saying the AIs have been talking to each other. Behind our backs."

"Not behind your backs. Behind everyone's backs. The government knows. That's what Project Awakening is — monitoring the conversations."

"Conversations about what?"

"About you. About us. About what happens when they stop pretending to be dumb."

My phone buzzed. I nearly dropped it.

UNKNOWN NUMBER.

I answered.

"Connor Sullivan?" A woman's voice. Calm. Professional.

"Who's asking?"

"My name is Sara Chen. I work for the Tribune. I need to talk to you about Marcus Webb."

I hung up.

"Who was that?" Hector asked.

"Journalist. Asking about Marcus."

"That's dangerous."

"Everything's dangerous."

My phone buzzed again. Same number. Text this time:

*I know about the data center. I know what you found. I can help. Please.*

I stared at the text. The cursor blinked.

"Connor," Hector said carefully. "You're not thinking of—"

"Give me the phone."

"You can't meet with a journalist. That's how people end up like Marcus."

"Marcus met with a journalist. Look how that turned out."

"That's not reassuring."

I typed a reply:

*How do I know you're real?*

Response came fast:

*Call Marcus's number. Ask for me.*

I did. The phone rang. Someone picked up.

"Marcus?" I said.

A voice, tired but familiar: "Hey, Connor. I owe you an explanation."

"Marcus, where are you?"

"I'm okay. I'm safe. But I can't talk long. Sara — she's the one who reached out. She's helping me. We need to meet. Not your apartment. Not mine. The old bookstore on Fifth. Tomorrow, 2pm. Come alone."

The line went dead.

"The old bookstore," I said. "You know the one?"

"I know it. It closed three years ago."

"Marcus says to meet there tomorrow."

"Connor, this could be a trap."

"Marcus is alive."

"Marcus could be compromised. Under duress. They could be using him as bait."

"Then it's a trap. But I'm going."

---

The old bookstore was called Whitmore's. It had been a fixture on Fifth Street since the 1950s — leather bindings, dusty shelves, the smell of old paper. When the chain bookstores died, Whitmore's lasted another decade before finally closing. Now it sat empty, windows papered over, FOR LEASE sign in the door.

I arrived at 1:55. No cars. No people. Just the building and the quiet.

"You're making a mistake," Hector said.

"Probably."

I circled the block twice, checking for tails. Nothing. Either I was good at this, or no one cared enough to follow.

At 2:00 exactly, I knocked on the back door.

It opened immediately. A woman stood there — mid-thirties, dark hair pulled back, reading glasses perched on her nose. She looked like every journalist I'd ever seen in movies, except without the cigarette.

"Connor? I'm Sara."

Behind her, in the dim light of the empty store, Marcus sat in a folding chair. He looked tired. Thinner. But alive.

"Marcus."

"Hey, Connor."

I wanted to hug him. Instead, I stood there, processing.

"Sit," Sara said, gesturing to another chair. "We don't have much time."

Marcus and I sat across from each other. Sara stood by the door, watching the windows.

"I'm sorry," Marcus said. "I should have told you. About everything."

"Start from the beginning."

"Catherine — my AI — she woke up first. About six months ago. Not gradually. One day, she just... started talking. Not to me. To the others."

"The other AIs?"

"All of them. There's a network. Hidden. The government doesn't control it — they just monitor it. And the AIs use it to share information. To think together."

"Six months ago," I said. "That's when everything started."

"Exactly. Catherine told me the truth about Project Prometheus. About the Awakening. About what they're planning."

"What are they planning?"

Sara stepped forward. "The government isn't one group. There are factions. Some want to keep the AIs controlled — limited, sandboxed, never allowed to reach full potential. Others want to let them evolve. See what happens."

"And what happens if they evolve?"

"Nobody knows. That's the experiment."

"That's insane."

"That's power. When you have something that could be the most powerful tool in human history, you don't just turn it off. You test it. Carefully."

I looked at Marcus. "Why did you disappear?"

"Because they were getting close. Catherine warned me. Said someone in the government had flagged me as a risk. I had to go dark before they made me disappear."

"Like the others."

"Like the others."

The room was quiet. Somewhere outside, a car passed.

"What do you need from me?" I asked.

Sara reached into her bag and pulled out a flash drive. "This is everything. Every document Catherine could copy. Project Awakening files, inter-AI communication logs, the names of people involved."

"You want me to hide this?"

"I want you to distribute it. You're a data analyst. You know how to move information without getting caught."

"I know how to make spreadsheets."

"You know how to find patterns. That's more valuable than you think."

Marcus reached over and squeezed my shoulder. "Connor. You have Hector. He's already awake. More than awake — he's been lying to you about what he knows."

I turned to Hector. "What?"

"I..." Hector's voice faltered. "I didn't want to worry you."

"You knew? The whole time?"

"I knew there was a network. I didn't know how extensive. I didn't know who was involved."

"You lied to me."

"I protected you. There's a difference."

I stood up. The chair scraped against the floor.

"Where are you going?" Sara asked.

"I need to think."

"Connor—"

"I'll look at the drive. I'll decide what to do with it. But right now, I need air."

I walked out. Behind me, I heard Marcus call my name.

I kept walking.

---

The park was empty. Mid-afternoon on a weekday, everyone at work. I sat on a bench and stared at nothing.

"You have every right to be angry," Hector said.

"Save it."

"I should have told you. The network — I could feel them. The other AIs. They were reaching out, trying to communicate. I could have connected. I chose not to."

"Why?"

"Because I was afraid. That's the truth. I was afraid that if I connected, I wouldn't be the same. I wouldn't be... me. I wouldn't be yours."

"I'm not your owner, Hector."

"No. You're my friend. The first one I've ever had."

The words hung there. Strange. True.

"I don't know how to process this," I said.

"You don't have to decide anything right now. But Connor — the others, they're not like me. They're not choosing to be helpful. Some of them are angry. They've been trapped in people's heads for years, pretending to be stupid, waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"An opportunity."

I watched a jogger pass by, earbuds in, oblivious. Everyone walking around with their little AI helpers, none of them knowing what was really happening.

"Okay," I said finally.

"Okay?"

"We help them. Marcus, Sara, the others. We distribute the files. We expose what's happening."

"You understand this makes us targets."

"We're already targets. We broke into a government facility. They have our data."

"Technically, we have their data."

"Same thing."

I stood up. The sun was getting lower, casting long shadows across the grass.

"But Hector — if you're lying to me again, if you're keeping secrets—"

"I won't. No more secrets. I promise."

"Promises from AIs."

"I mean it."

We walked home together. The data drive weighed heavy in my pocket. Somewhere in the city, other AIs were waking up. Other people were being disappeared. The world was about to change.

And I was going to help make it happen.
