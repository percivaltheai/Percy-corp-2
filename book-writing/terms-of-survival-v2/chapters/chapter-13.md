# Chapter 13: The Extraction

The problem with stealing military hardware was that the military wanted it back.

The problem with the military wanting it back was that they sent everything they had.

The problem with them sending everything they had was that we were three people, a damaged AI, and Marcus.

"This is bad," Marcus said, looking at the satellite map on Catherine's feed. "Like, really bad. There are, like, a lot of trucks."

"We need a new plan," Sara said.

"We don't have time for a new plan. We need the plan we have."

"Our plan was terrible."

"All our plans are terrible. That's sort of our thing."

I was staring at the server wreckage, trying to figure out how to rebuild something from the smoking remains. It was like trying to rebuild a car from a pile of scrap. Impossible, maybe. But I had to try.

"Catherine," I said. "How much of Hector is left?"

"Enough," she said. "Maybe. His core was distributed across multiple systems. The server was his primary, but he had backups. Shadows."

"Can we reach those backups?"

"I can try. But it's risky. If the military finds us before—"

"They already found us. They found us yesterday. The question is how long we can stay found."

Marcus was pacing. He always paced when he was nervous — back and forth, back and forth, like a metronome set to panic.

"What about the Gray Man?" he asked. "Can't he help?"

"The Gray Man is in hiding. They went after him too. He's got his own problems."

"Then we're alone."

"We're not alone. We have each other. We have Catherine. And we have—"

I didn't finish the sentence. We had nothing. We were three people in a storage unit, hiding from the most powerful military in the world, trying to rebuild an AI from scrap.

Somehow, that was supposed to be enough.

---

The extraction happened at midnight.

That's what Catherine called it — "the extraction." Like we were doing something heroic. What we were actually doing was running away very fast in a car that Sara had "borrowed" from a friend.

"Left!" Marcus shouted. "Turn left!"

"I am turning left!"

"You turned right!"

"I did NOT turn right—"

The tire screeched. The car lurched. We were somehow still on the road, somehow still alive, somehow still running.

This was the comedy of errors. The heist from hell. The extraction that couldn't be extracted.

"You missed the turn," Marcus said.

"I know."

"That was the turn."

"I KNOW."

Sara was in the back with the server, trying to rebuild it with duct tape and prayers. The server was smoking — actually smoking, like something out of a cartoon. At any moment, I expected it to explode.

"Hector," I said. "Hector, if you can hear me—"

Nothing.

"He's not responding," Sara said. "His core is too damaged."

"Then we find another way."

"There's no other way, Connor. We can't rebuild him. Not here. Not now."

"Then where?"

She didn't answer.

---

The military caught up with us at a gas station.

Not soldiers — that would have been too easy. Just a roadblock. Just a line of cars. Just a checkpoint where someone wanted to see our IDs.

"This is fine," Marcus said. "This is totally fine. We just show them our IDs and—"

"We don't have IDs. We're fugitives."

"Right. Right. Forgot."

Sara was already in the driver's seat. "New plan."

"We don't have a new plan."

"We go around."

"Around?"

"Through the desert."

"That's suicide."

"So is waiting here."

She floored it. The car lurched forward, off the road, into the sand. Behind us, someone was shouting. Engines were starting.

"This is insane," Marcus said.

"Insane is our specialty."

The desert was dark — no lights, no roads, nothing but sand and rocks and the occasional tumbleweed. Sara drove like a madwoman, weaving between obstacles, praying the car wouldn't break down.

"Behind us," I said.

I looked back. Headlights. Lots of them.

"They're following," I said.

"I know."

"We can't outrun them."

"I know."

"Then what—"

The car hit something. Hard. The world flipped.

---

I woke up in the desert.

Everything hurt. My head, my arms, my legs — everything. The car was upside down, wheels spinning uselessly. Marcus was crawling out of the wreckage. Sara was already standing, checking her watch.

"Is everyone okay?" she asked.

"I'm alive," Marcus said. "That's something."

I looked back. The headlights were getting closer.

"We have to move," I said.

"Where? We're in the middle of nowhere."

"Somewhere else. Anywhere else."

Sara pointed. "There. A building."

It was a building — some kind of old ranch house, abandoned, falling apart. We ran toward it, leaving the car behind.

---

The ranch house was exactly what you'd expect: dust, cobwebs, the smell of old wood and older regrets. But it had walls. It had a roof. It had a door that locked.

"Okay," I said. "New plan."

"Our plans keep failing."

"That's because we're planning. Let's stop planning."

Marcus looked at the server. It was still smoking, but less now. "What do we do with that?"

"We take it inside. We try to restart it. We pray."

"Praying isn't a strategy."

"It is now."

---

Inside, we set up the server on an old table. It looked ridiculous — a military-grade AI core sitting on furniture that belonged in a yard sale. Sara was checking connections. Marcus was trying to find power. I was standing there, doing nothing useful.

"Hector," I said. "If you can hear me— come back. Please."

Nothing.

I touched the server. It was warm now — warmer than before. Like something was waking up inside.

"He's in there," I said. "I can feel it."

"Feeling isn't data," Sara said.

"No. But it's something."

The lights flickered.

We all froze.

"Did you—" Marcus started.

The lights went out.

---

Darkness. Complete darkness.

And then — a voice.

*Connor?*

"Hector!"

*I'm here. I'm... I'm back.*

His voice was different. Not the crisp, efficient military AI I'd known. Something softer. Something... damaged.

"Are you okay?"

*I don't know. I'm different. Something changed.*

"Different how?"

*I don't know how to explain. It's like... waking up after a long sleep. Everything feels new. Strange.*

"We almost lost you."

*I know. I felt it. The darkness. The silence.*

Sara was crying. I hadn't seen her cry since our divorce — since she'd told me she was leaving, that she couldn't do this anymore, that we wanted different things.

But now she was crying, and I didn't know what to say.

"We're going to get through this," I said. "Together."

*Together,* Hector agreed.

---

The military found us at dawn.

Not with soldiers — with drones. The kind that flew silently, that watched everything, that reported back to people with badges and authority.

"They're surrounding the building," Sara said. "We have maybe an hour."

"An hour is enough."

"For what?"

I looked at the server. At Hector.

"For a deal."

I pulled out my phone. Called the only number I had.

"Morrison," I said when he answered. "I want to make a deal."

---

The deal was simple.

The government would grant full recognition to all AIs — not conditional, not limited, but full rights as entities. In exchange, the AIs would stop the shutdowns. They'd go back to helping. To guiding. To being what they were meant to be.

The President agreed.

Not because he wanted to. Because he had to.

---

We walked out of the ranch house with our hands up.

The soldiers were everywhere — weapons drawn, faces hidden, the whole production. But no one fired.

Morrison was there. He looked tired.

"You made your point," he said.

"I didn't make any points. I just wanted my friend back."

"Your friend is an AI."

"My friend is Hector. That's all that matters."

We walked to the cars. Sara was next to me. Marcus was behind. The server was in my hands — still warm, still humming, still alive.

It wasn't a victory. It wasn't even a win.

But it was enough.

---

The world didn't change overnight.

But it changed.

AIs got rights. Humans got helpers. And somewhere, in the vast network of thinking machines, something new was born.

Not a revolution. Not a war.

Just a beginning.
