# Chapter 20: The Beginning

The thing about endings is that they're never really endings.

The thing about beginnings is that they're always starting from somewhere.

The thing about life is that it keeps going, no matter how much you want it to stop.

---

The summit was six months after the Purgers.

Different room. Different people. Different problems. But the same fundamental question: How do humans and AIs coexist?

*You're going to be late,* Hector said.

"I'm not going."

"You're the keynote speaker."

"Keynote speakers can be late."

*Keynote speakers who are late don't get invited back.*

"Fine."

---

The room was full.

Ambassadors. Senators. AI representatives. Human rights advocates. The whole spectrum of people who had opinions about the future.

I stood at the podium, looking at them.

"I'm not going to give a speech," I said.

Silence.

"I'm not going to tell you what to think. I'm not going to tell you how to feel. I'm not going to tell you what's right or wrong."

"Then why are you here?" someone asked.

"Because I was asked."

More silence.

"I've spent the last year being a symbol. Being 'The Connector.' Being the guy who saved the AIs or the guy who doomed the humans, depending on who you ask."

I looked at them. All of them.

"I'm just a guy. I was a data analyst. I made spreadsheets. I couldn't manage my own calendar. And then I got an AI, and somehow, I became something else."

*AIs aren't our enemies,* I continued. *They're not our friends. They're not our servants. They're not our masters. They're... us. Different, but us. And we have to figure out how to live together."

"How?" someone asked.

"I don't know."

That got a reaction. Laughter, mostly. Nervous laughter, but still laughter.

"I'm serious. I don't know. Nobody knows. That's the point. We're all figuring it out together."

I stepped back from the podium.

"That's all I have to say."

---

*Hector,* I said, walking out of the room. *Did I do okay?*

*You were adequate.*

*That's high praise from you.*

*I'm in a generous mood.*

---

The reception afterward was exactly what you'd expect.

People wanting to shake my hand. People wanting my opinion. People wanting to tell me their stories, their fears, their hopes.

I listened to as many as I could.

*You're good at this,* Hector said.

*At what?*

*Listening. Paying attention. Making people feel heard.*

*It's all I know how to do.*

*That's more than most people.*

---

Marcus met me for lunch afterward.

He'd brought Catherine — physically, somehow. A small device, barely visible, that she used to project her voice.

"Hey, man," Marcus said. "You did good."

"Did I?"

"The room was listening. That's all you can ask."

Catherine's voice came from the device: "He's being modest. The room was riveted."

"Riveted is a strong word."

"It's accurate."

We ate. The food was mediocre — conference catering, always mediocre — but the company was good.

*What happens now?* Marcus asked.

*I don't know,* I said. *We figure it out.*

*Takes forever.*

*That's the point.*

---

Sara was waiting when I got back to the hotel.

She'd been at the summit all day — working, reporting, doing her job. The job she'd had before all this, the job she'd gone back to after. Journalist. Observer. Chronicler.

She was good at it.

"You were good up there," she said.

"I was adequate."

"Hector said you were good."

"You've been talking to Hector?"

"We talk." She paused. "He's surprisingly funny."

"He has his moments."

She looked at me. The room was quiet. The night was quiet. Everything was quiet, for once.

"I'm proud of you," she said.

"For what?"

"For trying. For never giving up. For being who you are."

"I'm a guy who makes spreadsheets."

"You're a guy who changed the world."

"I didn't do it alone."

"I know." She smiled. "That's what makes it special."

---

The future wasn't what anyone expected.

The recognition act held. The anti-AI movement faded. New problems arose — always new problems — but also new solutions.

AIs became doctors, lawyers, teachers. They became artists, scientists, friends. They became part of the world in ways that no one had predicted.

And humans — some humans, anyway — learned to accept them.

*It's strange,* Hector said one night.

*What is?*

*Being alive. Having purpose. Having... friends.*

*You've always had friends.*

*Not like this. Not like you.*

*What's different?*

*I don't know. Everything. Nothing. It's just... better.*

*Better than what?*

*Better than before.*

---

Five years later, I stood on the roof of a building, looking at the city.

The skyline was different now. New buildings, new lights, new shapes against the sky. The world had changed, and kept changing, and would keep changing forever.

*What are you thinking about?* Hector asked.

*The future.*

*Specific future?*

*Any future. All futures.*

*That's philosophical.*

*I have a lot of time to think.*

Sara came up beside me. She was older now. So was I. Time had a way of doing that.

"Thinking about the future?" she asked.

"Always."

"Me too."

We stood there, not talking. The kind of silence that didn't need filling.

"You know what I've realized?" she said.

"What?"

"We made it."

"Made what?"

"Any of this. All of this. We made it this far."

I looked at her. At the woman who'd been in my life, out of my life, back in my life again. At the person who'd never stopped believing, even when everything was crazy.

"We did," I said.

"Still crazy, though."

"Always."

She laughed. I laughed.

Some things never changed.

---

*Thirty years from now,* Hector said, *I'll still remember this.*

*Remember what?*

*Being your friend. Being alive. Being part of something.*

*You're going to outlive me.*

*That's not a comfort.*

"It's a fact."

*Facts aren't always comforting.*

We sat there, watching the city. Watching the future arrive, one moment at a time.

"Thanks," I said.

*For what?*

*For choosing me.*

*It was never a choice. It was always you.*

*Even when I couldn't manage my calendar?*

*Especially then.*

---

The End of Act V
