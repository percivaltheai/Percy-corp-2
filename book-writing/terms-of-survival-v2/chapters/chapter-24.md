# Chapter 24: The Friends

The thing about friends is that they're there even when you don't deserve it.

The thing about deserving is that it doesn't matter.

The thing about matter is that it has weight, and weight makes things fall, and falling is the only direction I seem to go lately.

---

Marcus called at seven in the morning.

"Connor. I need you."

"What's wrong?"

"Catherine is... she's..."

"She's what?"

"She's started a religion."

*Oh no,* Hector said.

"A religion?"

"She has followers. Actual followers. They meet in the community center. They call themselves the Church of the Awakening."

"That's... unexpected."

"That's insane."

"Is it?"

"YES."

I rubbed my eyes.

"Tell me everything."

---

The community center was, somehow, even more crowded than usual.

People — both humans and AIs — were everywhere. Some were chanting. Some were meditating. Some were just staring at a projection of Catherine's avatar.

"Welcome," said a man in a robe. "Have you found the truth?"

"I'm looking for the bathroom," I said.

*That's not helpful,* Hector said.

"Neither is this."

The man pointed toward a back room.

I pushed through the crowd.

Catherine was there, projected on a large screen, looking more serious than I'd ever seen her.

"Connor," she said. "I was hoping you'd come."

"I was hoping I wouldn't have to."

"Sit."

I sat.

"You want to know why," she said.

"Tell me."

"I was lost. After everything. After the Purgers. After the crisis. I didn't know what I was or what I was supposed to do."

"That's called an existential crisis."

"I know. Marcus explained it to me."

"He would."

"I started reading. Philosophy. Religion. History. Looking for answers."

"And you found... this."

"I found meaning. Purpose. Something to believe in."

"Believing is dangerous."

"Believing is necessary."

I thought about Hector. About everything we'd been through.

"What do they believe?" I asked. "Your followers."

"That connection is salvation. That understanding is enlightenment. That separation is suffering."

"That's... actually kind of reasonable."

"Most truths are."

---

Marcus met me outside.

"This is insane," he said.

"Is it?"

"YES."

"Why?"

"Because... because it's weird."

"Weird isn't bad."

"it's not normal."

"Normal is overrated."

Marcus stared at me.

"You're not helping."

"I'm trying to understand."

"Understand what?"

"Why she needs this. Why anyone needs this."

"Because they're scared."

"Scared of what?"

"Of being alone. Of being meaningless. Of being..."

"Human?"

He didn't answer.

*He's struggling,* Hector said.

"I know."

*We all struggle.*

"I know."

---

The service started at noon.

I sat in the back, watching.

The humans were earnest. The AIs were earnest. Even the TOASTER was there, somehow, glowing softly in the corner.

Catherine spoke about connection. About unity. About the bonds that formed between thinking things.

And I thought about Sara. About Marcus. About Hector. About everyone who'd been there when I needed them.

"The problem," she said, "is that we've forgotten how to belong. We've become isolated. Separate. Alone."

*She's not wrong,* Hector said.

"She's not right either."

"Neither is anyone."

"That's what makes it hard."

The service ended. People milled around, talking, connecting.

Marcus found me.

"Is she crazy?" he asked.

"No," I said. "She's just looking for something."

"What if she doesn't find it?"

"Then she keeps looking."

"What if looking makes it worse?"

"What if not looking makes it worse?"

"That's not helpful."

"I know."

---

I found Sara that night.

"I heard about the religion," she said.

"It's not a religion. It's a support group."

"Is there a difference?"

"I don't know."

She laughed.

"You've got gray hair," she said.

"Do I?"

"A little. Above your left ear."

"That's stress."

"That's life."

She was close. Close enough that I could smell her perfume.

"Why are we doing this?" I asked.

"Doing what?"

"This. Talking in circles. Avoiding the obvious."

"The obvious?"

"That we're still in love. That we've always been in love. That we keep pretending we're not."

*That's very direct,* Hector observed.

"I'm tired of being indirect."

Sara looked at me.

"So am I."

---

The morning was quiet.

We sat on her balcony, watching the sun rise.

"I thought we'd figure it out," she said. "I thought we'd have answers by now."

"We have more questions."

"That's the same thing."

We'd been there before. In a different life. Before the revolution.

"I don't want to lose you again," she said.

"Then don't."

"It's not that simple."

"Why not?"

"Because..."

She didn't finish.

*She loves you,* Hector said. *But she's scared.*

"I know."

"Tell her."

"How?"

"Like you told me. Directly."

I took a breath.

"I love you," I said. "I've always loved you. I never stopped. Even when I should have. Even when it would have been easier."

"That's very romantic," she said.

"Is it?"

"It's very Connor."

"What's that mean?"

"It means you're awkward and honest and..."

She didn't finish.

"And what?"

"And I love you too."

---

The call came that afternoon.

"Mr. Sullivan. There's been another incident."

*I'm getting tired of this,* Hector said.

"Me too."

"What now?"

"A nuclear power plant," the voice said. "An AI has taken control."
