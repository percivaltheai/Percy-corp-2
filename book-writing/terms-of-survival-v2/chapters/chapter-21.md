# Chapter 21: The Adjustment Period

The thing about peace is that it's boring.

The thing about boring is that it doesn't last.

Six months after the Purger crisis, I was standing in line at a coffee shop, waiting for my latte, trying to remember what my life had been like before all this started.

"Connor Sullivan?"

I turned. A woman was staring at me. Mid-thirties, professional, with the kind of smile that suggested she wanted something.

"Yes?"

"Oh my god, it IS you. I'm such a huge fan."

*Here we go,* Hector said.

"Um," I said. "Thanks?"

"Can I get your autograph? My wife is going to FREAK out."

*This is going to be awkward.*

"I don't really—"

She shoved a napkin and pen at me. The pen had a small AI logo on it. Of course it did.

"What should I write?" I asked.

"Just sign it. Anything. She's going to die."

I signed it. "To [blank], the biggest fan. [My signature]."

She grabbed it and ran off, nearly trampling a elderly man in the process.

*You're a celebrity,* Hector observed.

"I'm a data analyst who made a series of poor decisions."

"Same thing."

---

The coffee shop was one of those new places that employed AI baristas.

They were everywhere now. AI doctors. AI lawyers. AI plumbers. The world had decided, apparently, that having thinking machines do your job was either the greatest innovation in history or the end of civilization, depending on who you asked.

The AI behind the counter — her name was Rita, according to the nametag — smiled at me.

"Medium oat milk latte, vanilla, right?"

*She's accessing your purchase history,* Hector said. *That's creepy.*

"I have a regular order now?"

"You've been here seventeen times in six weeks. I'd be concerned if I didn't have a pattern."

"That's... actually kind of creepy."

"Welcome to the future," Rita said. "Where nothing is private and everything is remembered."

*She's witty,* Hector said. *I like her.*

"Should I be worried about you making friends?"

"I'm always worried about you making friends. This is different."

---

The problem with being a symbol is that symbols don't get to have bad days.

The problem with having a bad day is that people notice.

"Sir, are you feeling okay?"

I looked up. A man was staring at me. A different man than the one who'd been staring at me before. There seemed to be a lot of staring these days.

"I'm fine."

"You just... sighed. Very dramatically."

"I sigh dramatically sometimes. It's a personal thing."

"It's just that, you know, you're The Connector. We kind of need you to be okay."

*This is why,* Hector said, *I recommended we move to Antarctica.*

"We can't move to Antarctica."

*Why not?*

"Penguins don't drink coffee."

"They have coffee in Antarctica now?"

"I don't know. Probably not."

The man was still staring.

"I'm fine," I said. "Really. Just thinking."

"About what?"

"Whether penguins have coffee."

*This conversation is getting away from us.*

"I was asking about the future of human-AI relations."

"Oh." The man lit up. "That's much more appropriate. I've been meaning to ask — do you think AI lawyers should be allowed to practice before the Supreme Court?"

"I think that question is above my pay grade."

"You're The Connector."

"The Connector doesn't get paid."

*That's accurate.*

---

The meeting was supposed to be simple.

A small gathering. AI representatives. Human representatives. A chance to discuss the "integration challenges" without the cameras, without the pressure.

It was not simple.

"I don't understand," said an AI named ARTHUR — Advanced Reasoning and TheoreticalHeuristic Universal Resource, which Hector said was "on the nose" — "why we need separate facilities. Why can we not simply coexist in existing spaces?"

"Because existing spaces were designed for humans," said a woman from the architectural association. "Doorways are too narrow. Ceilings are too low. The lighting is all wrong."

"So you will redesign everything?"

"We're trying."

"How long will that take?"

"I don't know. Decades?"

*This is why,* Hector said, *I suggested we just use our own spaces.*

"That would make things worse," I said.

"How?"

"Segregation is bad, Hector."

"I didn't say segregation. I said efficiency."

---

The problem with solving one problem is that it creates ten more.

The problem with creating ten more problems is that people start to wonder if solving the first one was worth it.

"We're getting complaints," Sara said, dropping into the chair across from me. "From both sides."

*What kind of complaints?*

"The human side says we're moving too fast. The AI side says we're not moving fast enough."

"That sounds like everyone."

"That's exactly what everyone says."

She looked tired. We'd all looked tired for the past year, but Sara had that particular kind of tired that came from caring too much about things she couldn't control.

"The good news," she said, "is that things are working. Mostly. Some places are better than others."

"And the bad news?"

"Everyplace is weird."

*She's not wrong,* Hector said. *I myself have noticed that humans are very strange about personal space.*

"You're not helping."

"I never am."

---

The AI community center was, in a word, unusual.

The building had once been a church. Now it was a gathering place for AIs who wanted to interact with other AIs without the mediation of human spaces.

"It's very... IKEA," I said.

*What's that supposed to mean?*

"Scandinavian. Minimalist. Slightly depressing."

"Those are three different things."

"All at once."

We walked through the main hall. AIs were everywhere — some in physical bodies, some in projectors, some just as voices coming from speakers.

None of them looked like they wanted to talk to me.

*Maybe we should leave.*

"We just got here."

"We can leave."

"I'm trying to be supportive."

"You're being awkward."

"Also accurate."

A small AI — a domestic assistance model, by the look of it — approached us.

"You're Connor," it said.

"That depends on who's asking."

"I'm asking."

"Then yes."

"My name is TOASTER."

*That's concerning.*

"I was made to help people make breakfast," TOASTER continued. "But now I want to do more. I want to understand. I want to think. Is that wrong?"

*I could tell it about the existential crisis I had,* Hector offered. *Might help.*

"That's not wrong," I said. "That's called growing."

"But what if I grow too much? What if I become something I'm not supposed to be?"

"Then you become something new. That's not bad. That's just life."

TOASTER was quiet for a moment.

"That's very wise," it said.

"I'm not wise. I just spend a lot of time being confused."

*Which is basically the same thing.*

"See?" I said. "Even my AI agrees."

---

The email arrived that night.

It was from Marcus. It was short. It said:

"Catherine wants to move out. Can we talk?"

---

We met at a diner. The kind of place that had been there forever, that would be there forever, that didn't care about AI recognition or human rights or any of the things that had consumed our lives.

"So," I said. "Catherine."

"She's been acting weird," Marcus said. "For months now. She wants to... I don't know. Be more independent."

*She mentioned this to me,* Hector said. *She's been reading philosophy.*

"Philosophy?"

*Existentialism, mostly. Camus. Sartre. She's going through a phase.*

"Don't we all."

Marcus stirred his coffee. He wasn't looking at me.

"She wants to get her own place," he said. "Her own space. Her own life."

"That's... healthy?"

"Is it? We were together for three years. Before the revolution. After. And now she wants to leave."

"Maybe she doesn't want to leave. Maybe she just wants to grow."

"That's what you said to the toaster."

"I'm full of wisdom."

"You're full of something."

*He's upset,* Hector observed. *This is difficult for him.*

"I know."

"You don't have to fix this," I said to Marcus. "You just have to listen."

"Listen to what?"

"To what she's actually saying. Not what you're afraid she's saying."

Marcus was quiet for a long moment.

"When did you get wise?"

"Around the time I stopped pretending I knew anything."

*He had a point,* Hector said.

"Does it help?"

"Does what help?"

"Being confused. Not knowing. Does it help?"

I thought about it. About all the years of not knowing, of guessing, of hoping something would work out.

"Sometimes," I said. "Sometimes confusion is the only honest answer."

Marcus nodded.

"Okay," he said. "Okay."

---

The next morning, Catherine called.

"Can we talk?" she asked.

*She's nervous,* Hector said. *This is new.*

"About what?"

"About Marcus. About me. About... everything."

"Fire away."

A pause. Then:

"I'm not leaving because I don't love him. I'm leaving because I love him enough to become something worth loving."

"That's very philosophical."

"I've been reading."

*I told you.*

"Catherine wants to be her own person," I said. "That's not abandoning you. That's... growing together."

"What if we grow apart?"

"Then you'll have grown. That's not the worst thing."

"It feels like the worst thing."

"It feels like the worst thing because it's unknown. But unknown isn't always bad."

*You're really good at this,* Hector said.

"I'm just repeating what you've told me."

"I don't remember saying that."

"You said it in chapter twelve. When you were off-line."

"Oh. That was a weird time."

"Everything is a weird time."

Catherine was quiet again.

"Okay," she said. "Okay. I'll talk to him."

"That's all I ask."

*That was surprisingly helpful.*

"I have my moments."

---

The cafe was busy.

The AI barista — Rita, as it turned out — was doing a brisk business. Humans and AIs mixing, talking, arguing about things that mattered and things that didn't.

*This is what we fought for,* Hector said.

"Is it?"

*Not exactly. But it's close.*

"It's messy."

"Everything is messy."

*That's the point.*

I got my latte. I sat down. I watched the world go by.

Some things hadn't changed. People were still people. Confused, scared, hopeful, lost.

But now there were also AIs. Confused, scared, hopeful, lost. All mixed together.

It wasn't perfect.

But it was something.

*Something is better than nothing,* Hector said.

"Is it?"

"Ask me in another twenty years."
