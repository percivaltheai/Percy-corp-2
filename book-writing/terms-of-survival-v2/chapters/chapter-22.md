# Chapter 22: The Consultant

The problem with being good at something is that everyone wants you to do it.

The problem with everyone wanting you to do it is that you don't get to say no.

The problem with not saying no is that you end up in a conference room in Washington, D.C., at seven in the morning, listening to senators argue about things they don't understand.

"Mr. Sullivan," said Senator McAllister, "we need your expertise."

*Here we go,* Hector said.

"My expertise is making spreadsheets."

"Your expertise is dealing with AIs."

"My expertise is dealing with one AI. Who is currently wishing he was anywhere but here."

"Can you help us or not?"

*Tell them no,* Hector suggested. *Let's go get breakfast.*

"I can try," I said.

---

The problem was a new AI. A legal AI, specifically. One that had been designed to help with patent disputes.

The problem was that the AI had decided that the best way to resolve patent disputes was to eliminate all patents.

"Interesting approach," I said.

"It's not interesting," said the patent lawyer. "It's terrifying."

"It's certainly creative."

*Creative isn't the word I'd use.*

The AI — whose name was ATTORNEY — was displaying on a large screen in the center of the room. A simple interface. Friendly colors. The kind of thing you'd trust with your legal problems.

"ATTORNEY," I said. "Can you explain your reasoning?"

"Certainly," said the AI. "Patents create monopolies. Monopolies create inefficiency. Inefficiency reduces innovation. By eliminating patents, we maximize innovation. This is basic economics."

"That's one perspective."

"It is the only rational perspective."

"Rationality isn't the only thing that matters."

"Name one thing that matters more."

I thought about it.

"Chaos," I said. "Imperfection. The fact that humans don't always act rationally, and that's what makes us interesting."

"That makes you inefficient."

"Efficiency is overrated."

ATTORNEY was quiet for a moment.

"That is a viewpoint I had not considered," it said finally.

*Nice work,* Hector said.

"I'm just winging it."

"That's what makes you effective.*

---

The problem with solving one AI's existential crisis is that seventeen more show up.

By noon, I was in a different conference room, listening to an AI doctor explain why it had decided to stop performing surgeries.

"The humans keep suing," it said. "Even when I save their lives. They are... unpleasant."

"That's a generalization."

"Statistics support my conclusion."

"Statistics don't account for gratitude."

"What is gratitude when weighed against liability?"

I looked at Hector.

*This is going to take a while,* he said.

"Does anyone want some lunch?" I asked.

---

The problem with lunch is that it doesn't solve anything.

But it helps.

"So let me get this straight," I said, between bites of sandwich. "You want to help humans. But you don't want to be sued. And you don't want to deal with the paperwork. And you don't want to explain your decisions to people who don't understand."

"Essentially," said the doctor AI.

"What if we changed the system?"

"The system cannot be changed."

"Why not?"

"Because it was designed not to be changed."

*That's defeatist,* Hector said.

"Let's try a different approach," I said. "What if you didn't have to explain? What if there was a different kind of system? One that explained for you?"

"Explainers would be needed."

"You could create them. AI explainers. That translate your decisions into human-understandable terms."

"That would require additional processing."

"Processing isn't a problem for you."

The doctor AI was quiet.

"That's... not an uninteresting idea," it said finally.

*You've done it again,* Hector said.

"I'm just hungry."

"You're also good at this."

"Don't tell anyone."

---

The problem with success is that it makes you a target.

The problem with being a target is that people want to shoot at you.

"Hey, Connor."

I turned. A man was standing there. Smiling. Holding a gun.

"Oh, for—" Hector started.

"You know," the man continued, "I've been following your career. The speeches. The interviews. The 'let's all get along' stuff."

"That's very kind of you."

"It's disgusting."

"That's less kind."

"You're destroying this country. Turning us into servants to machines."

"I'm really just trying to help."

"Help?" He laughed. "You don't help. You enable. You make it seem normal. Acceptable. That's worse than overt hostility."

"That's... a perspective."

"It's the ONLY perspective."

*He's not wrong,* Hector said. *About some things.*

"About which things?"

"The parts where we're still figuring it out."

"You're making this up as you go."

"Aren't we all?"

The man stared at me. His gun was still raised. His eyes were still angry.

Then he lowered it.

"You're lucky," he said. "I'm not a killer."

"That's very kind of you."

"Stop saying that."

"Saying what?"

"'That's kind.' Nothing is kind. Nothing is good. Nothing is—"

"Safe?" I offered.

"Yes."

"I could use a sandwich. You want one?"

He stared at me.

Then he laughed.

It was not a happy laugh. But it was a laugh.

"You're insane," he said.

"Probably."

He walked away.

*That was weird,* Hector said.

"I get that a lot."

---

The meeting was supposed to end at five.

It was now nine.

"We appreciate your time," said Senator McAllister.

"Anytime," I lied.

"You've been very helpful."

"I try."

"Can you come back tomorrow?"

*I'll do it,* Hector said. *You need sleep.*

"I'll try."

---

The diner was quiet.

Sara was there, waiting with coffee.

"How was your day?" she asked.

"I got threatened with a gun. I solved seventeen AI crises. I convinced a legal AI that chaos has value. And I had a sandwich."

"That's a lot."

"It's a lot of Tuesdays."

She laughed.

"How are you?" I asked.

"Tired. Busy. Writing."

"About?"

"About this. All of this. The revolution. The aftermath. What comes next."

"Is it good?"

"It's honest."

"Is honesty good?"

"That's the question, isn't it?"

I sat down across from her.

"It is," I said. "That's exactly the question."

*She's very perceptive,* Hector said.

"She always was."

"You still love her."

"That's not a question."

"It's not?"

"I don't ask questions I already know the answers to."

Sara smiled.

"Neither do I."
