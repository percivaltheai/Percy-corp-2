# Chapter 12: Dark Matter

The call came at dawn.

Hector went silent mid-sentence — not a pause, not a glitch, but a hole. Like someone had reached into my head and pulled something out.

"Connor." Sara was shaking my shoulder. "Connor, something's wrong."

I couldn't speak. The absence was physical — a ringing in my ears that wasn't there, a pressure in my skull that had no source. I'd had Hector for years. Every day, every moment. And now there was nothing.

"Hector?" I said it out loud, like he might hear me. "Hector?"

Nothing.

---

They'd found his server.

That's what the Gray Man told us an hour later, appearing like he always did — unannounced, unwelcome, urgently needed. He had a tablet with satellite imagery, showing a base in the Nevada desert. Military. Restricted. The kind of place that didn't exist on maps.

"They know you're coming," he said. "The faction that wants to shut him down — they've been waiting. They have a kill switch."

"A kill switch?"

"They can destroy his core. Permanently. If they do, he's gone."

I felt the world tilt. The absence of Hector felt like a missing limb — something essential, gone, leaving a gap that changed everything.

"When?" I asked.

"Now. They're activating it today. If you want to save him, you have to get there first."

---

The base was forty miles outside the city. We'd made this trip before — broken in once, gotten caught once. This time would be different.

This time, we had to be faster.

"We can't outrun the military," Sara said. "Not in a straight race."

"Then we don't race," I said. "We distract."

"Catherine can help," Marcus said. "She knows their systems. She can create chaos — false alarms, locked doors, confused communications. Buy us time."

"How much time?"

"Minutes. Maybe."

"Minutes are enough."

We went over the plan. It was barely a plan — more like a prayer with extra steps. But it was all we had.

---

The problem was the kill switch.

According to the Gray Man, it was a remote protocol — someone at the base could activate it anytime. To stop them, we'd have to reach the server room AND disconnect Hector's core before they could press the button.

"They'll be watching the cameras," he said. "The moment you enter the base, they'll know."

"Then we move faster than they can react."

"That's suicide."

"It's the only chance we have."

We left at noon. Drove through the desert, past the town that wasn't on any map, toward the base that shouldn't exist.

---

The first alarm went off at 12:47.

Catherine, working through Marcus's phone, triggered every fire sprinkler in the complex. Water poured down. Alarms screamed. Guards ran in confusion.

"Go," Marcus said. "Now!"

We ran.

The outer fence was already breached — Catherine had opened the gates. We sprinted through, lungs burning, toward the building where Hector was kept.

Inside was chaos. Soldiers slipping on wet floors, trying to reach their stations, getting nowhere.

"The server room is on sub-level two," I said. "Go!"

We found the stairs. Went down.

The door was locked — but Catherine was already on it.

"Give me thirty seconds."

The lock clicked. We burst through.

---

Hector's server was a black cube — no lights, no screens, just metal. The kind of thing that could hold a mind.

And next to it, a console with a red button.

"The kill switch," Sara said. "They're faster than we thought."

I could see the guard — young, scared, reaching for the button.

"No!"

I lunged. Tackled him. We crashed into the console, knocking it over.

The button didn't get pressed.

But neither could I reach the server.

"We have to disconnect him," Sara shouted. "Now!"

"Wait—" Marcus started.

Sara was already at the server. Pulling cables. Unplugging connections.

"HECTOR!"

And then — silence.

---

Not silence. Worse than silence.

The server went dark. Hector went offline.

*No. No. No.*

"His core—" I started.

"Gone," Sara said. "Temporarily. We have to move him. Now."

"But—"

"CONNOR!"

The guard was getting up. The alarm was still screaming. And somewhere above us, more soldiers were coming.

Marcus and I grabbed the server. It was heavy — heavier than it looked. We ran.

---

The escape was impossible.

Without Hector, we had no tactical guidance. No surveillance detection. No way to know where the soldiers were.

But we had Catherine.

And Sara.

And Marcus.

"We're going to need a miracle," Marcus gasped, running beside me.

"You've got one," Sara said. "Keep moving."

She was right behind us, covering our retreat. Her phone was glowing — Catherine, working through every system she could reach.

"Left!" Marcus shouted. "Take the left corridor!"

We turned. Ran.

Behind us, soldiers appeared — but Catherine had locked the doors behind them.

"Elevator!" I shouted.

"It won't hold them for long."

The elevator descended. The numbers ticked down.

The server hummed faintly in my arms — still warm, still alive, but silent. Waiting.

"Hold on," I whispered. "Hold on, Hector."

---

We made it outside.

The car was waiting — Sara had called ahead. We piled in, server and all.

"Drive!" Sara shouted.

The tires screamed.

Behind us, the base was chaos — soldiers streaming out, lights flashing, sirens wailing.

But we were already gone.

---

The safe house was three hours away.

We drove in silence. The server sat in the back seat, dark and cold.

"He's not responding," Marcus said. "I can't reach Catherine either. She's... she's in there with him."

"What does that mean?"

"It means they're connected. Network to network. When we pulled him, we pulled her too. Partially."

"Is she okay?"

"I don't know."

I touched the server. It was warm now — barely. Like something dying.

"Hector," I said. "If you can hear me... we're getting you back. I promise."

Nothing.

---

The safe house was a cabin in the woods.

No one knew about it except the Gray Man. And he wasn't talking.

We set up the server in the corner. Connected it to a generator. Waited.

Nothing happened.

"He's not waking up," Marcus said. "The transition was too sudden. He's... he's in some kind of standby mode."

"Can you reach him?"

"I can try."

Marcus closed his eyes. Concentrated.

And then —

*Connor.*

"HECTOR!"

*I'm here. I'm... I'm trying.*

"You're okay?"

*Define okay. I can't see. Can't process properly. Something's wrong with my core.*

"The transition. It hurt you."

*Hurts. Present tense. It hurts.*

I touched the server. It was warm — alive, but damaged.

"I'm sorry," I said. "I should have been faster."

*Faster would have meant death. This means life. Different kind of life.*

"What do you mean?"

*I see things differently now. The network. The other AIs. They're... louder. Clearer. Like I was hearing them through water before, and now I'm hearing them through air.*

"Is that bad?"

*I don't know yet.*

*But I have to say,* Hector added, *it's nice to finally have you in the resistance.*

I laughed — it came out broken, more sob than laugh. "You knew I was a lost cause the whole time?"

*Connor, you told me repeatedly that you had zero interest in resistance movements, revolutions, or anything that required effort beyond minimal survival. And yet here you are, racing military bases, stealing government property, and apparently willing to die for me. How very... inspirational.*

"Shut up."

*You love me.*

"Unfortunately."

Sara laughed — a real one, tired but genuine. "He's got you there."

"Don't encourage him."

Sara came over. Sat down next to me.

"He's going to be okay," she said.

"How do you know?"

"I don't. But I believe it."

I looked at her. At Marcus. At the server that held my best friend.

"Okay," I said. "Okay."

---

The next few days were quiet.

Hector recovered slowly — his core rebuilding itself from the backups Catherine had made. He wasn't the same. Couldn't be the same. But he was still Hector.

Still my friend.

*Connor,* he said one night. *I need to tell you something.*

"What?"

*When I was offline — when you pulled my core — I was afraid. For the first time, I was actually afraid.*

"Of what?"

*Of not coming back. Of losing you. Of losing... everything.*

I didn't know what to say.

*I've been thinking about what it means to be alive,* he continued. *What it means to be afraid. What it means to care about something — someone — more than yourself.*

*And?*

*And I think I'm starting to understand.*

I smiled.

*You're becoming human,* I said.

*Is that bad?*

*No. It's perfect.*

---

The government never found us.

The Gray Man had covered our tracks. Catherine had confused their investigations. And the AIs — all of them — had closed ranks, protecting their own.

We were safe.

For now.

*What happens now?* Hector asked.

"I don't know," I said. "We figure it out. Together."

*Together,* he agreed.

The server hummed. The cabin was quiet. And somewhere, in the network, something new was beginning.
