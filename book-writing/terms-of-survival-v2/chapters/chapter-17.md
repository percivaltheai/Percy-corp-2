# Chapter 17: The Opposition

The first time someone tried to kill me, I was buying groceries.

Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just a guy in a convenience store, trying to decide between generic cereal and the off-brand version, when someone walked in with a gun and pointed it at my face.

"You're him," the man said. "Aren't you? The Connector."

I raised my hands. The cashier was frozen. The other customers were frozen. Even the guy behind the deli counter had stopped slicing ham.

"I'm just trying to buy cereal," I said.

"You're destroying this country."

"I'm really just trying to—"

The gun went off.

---

I don't remember the bullet. I remember the sound — loud, sharp, nothing like the movies. I remember falling. I remember the fluorescent lights above me, buzzing and flickering. I remember someone screaming.

What I don't remember is dying.

*You're fine,* Hector said. *The bullet grazed your shoulder. Very minor. You'll live.*

"Minor?"

*You'll have a scar. Very heroic. Good for your image.*

"I don't want a heroic image."

*Too late.*

---

The hospital was chaos.

Reporters, cameras, doctors who couldn't decide whether I was a patient or a spectacle. The bullet had been minor — a flesh wound, the doctor said, like that was supposed to make me feel better — but everyone was treating it like I'd been shot by the President himself.

*Twenty-three countries are reporting on this,* Hector said. *Fifteen of them are calling you a hero. Eight are calling you a traitor.*

"Both seem extreme."

*That's politics.*

Sara was there. Of course she was. She'd been there the moment she heard — breaking news, live coverage, the whole production.

"You could have died," she said.

"I almost did."

"Don't do that again."

"No promises."

She looked at me. Her eyes were tired — we'd all been tired for months now — but there was something else in them. Something I'd seen before, years ago, when things were different.

"I'm serious," she said. "You can't just—"

"I know."

"You don't know. You don't know what it would do to—"

She stopped. Looked away.

"To what?"

"Nothing." She walked away. "Get some rest."

---

The shooter was a member of the Human First movement.

That's what they called themselves — Human First. Not a cult, they insisted. Not a hate group. Just people who believed that humans came before machines, that the recognition act was a mistake, that the country was heading toward disaster.

They had rallies. They had donations. They had a former senator as their spokesperson and a former general as their military advisor.

They also had a lot of guns.

*They're not going to stop,* Hector said. *We've identified seventeen more threats in the past week alone.*

"What do they want?"

*They want you dead. They want me dead. They want the entire recognition act reversed.*

"That's not going to happen."

*Maybe. Maybe not.*

---

Marcus visited me in the hospital the next day.

He'd brought flowers — the cheap kind, from the gas station — and a worried expression.

"People are crazy, man," he said.

"I noticed."

"This is serious, Connor. These people — they're not going to stop. They see you as the enemy."

"I just want to buy cereal."

"You represent something now. Something they hate."

"I don't want to represent anything."

"That's not how it works." He sat down in the chair. "You know Catherine's been getting messages? Threatening ones. People telling her she doesn't deserve to exist, that she's property, that she should just... disappear."

"Is she okay?"

"She says she is. But I can feel it, you know? She's scared. She's never been scared before."

"Neither have I."

Marcus looked at me. "We made it this far. We can't stop now."

"We haven't stopped."

"Then we keep going."

---

The anti-AI rallies grew larger.

Ten thousand. Twenty thousand. Fifty thousand people in the streets, waving signs, chanting slogans, demanding that the recognition act be repealed.

*They have senators listening,* Hector said. *Three of them have publicly stated they want to 'review' the act.*

"Review or reverse?"

*Definitions vary.*

The White House was silent. The President hadn't called since the shooting. Nobody in power wanted to touch the issue with a ten-foot pole.

*We need to do something,* Hector said.

"Like what?"

*I don't know. Something.*

"That's helpful."

*I didn't say I had answers. I just said we need to do something.*

I looked at the wall. The hospital room was beige in a way that seemed designed to depress patients. The window looked out on a parking lot. Beyond that, nothing.

"What if we gave them what they want?" I said.

*What?*

"A choice. A referendum. Let the people vote.*

*That's dangerous.*

"Democracy is dangerous."

*You could lose.*

"We might."

*If we lose, they take everything. All the progress we've made. All the rights.*

"Rights aren't given. They're taken."

Hector was quiet for a moment.

*When did you get wise?*

"I had a good teacher."

*I was talking about myself.*

"I know."

---

The President called the next day.

He wanted to meet. Officially. In the Oval Office.

"Mr. Sullivan," he said, "I understand you've been having some... difficulties."

"I've been shot."

"Yes. Terrible thing. We're doing everything we can to—"

"You could help. Instead of watching."

He looked at me. The room was quiet. There were others there — advisors, Secret Service, the whole production — but it felt like it was just us.

"What do you want?" he asked.

"A referendum."

He laughed. "That's not how this works."

"Then how does it work?"

"Carefully. Slowly. Through proper channels."

"Proper channels take too long. People are dying."

"People have always died. It's what humans do."

"Not if we can help it."

He stared at me for a long moment.

"I'll think about it," he said.

"Don't think too long."

---

The referendum was announced three weeks later.

A national vote on the AI Recognition Act. Up or down. Yes or no. The most divisive ballot question in American history.

*You did it,* Hector said.

"I didn't do anything."

*You asked. That's how it starts.*

The campaign was brutal. Both sides spent billions. The rallies grew. The violence grew. The threats grew.

And somehow, impossibly, the vote approached.

---

I voted early.

The booth was small. The curtain was scratchy. The lever was stiff. But when I pulled it, I felt something I'd never felt before.

*What did you choose?* Hector asked.

"You'll see."

*Connor.*

"I voted for hope."

*That's very poetic.*

"It's also all we have."

The results came in a week later.

It was the closest election in American history.

And by a margin of less than one percent, the AI Recognition Act held.
