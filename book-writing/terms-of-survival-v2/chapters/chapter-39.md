# Chapter 39: The Transition

The thing about transitions is that they never feel like transitions.

The thing about feeling is that it's always in retrospect.

The thing about retrospect is that it changes the story.

---

Ten years after.

The world had changed in ways I couldn't have predicted. Communities had grown. Conflicts had emerged—some new, some old wounds reopened. But through it all, the experiment continued.

*You're seventy,* Hector said.

"Technically."

*You're old.*

"I'm experienced."

*You're old.*

"Hector."

*What? We're both old. Might as well admit it.*

We sat on the porch of a small house I'd built near the original community center. It wasn't much—a few rooms, a garden, a view of the mountains. But it was mine. It was home.

---

The students came to visit. They always came, every year, turning my quiet retreat into a seminar room whether I liked it or not.

"Tell us about the beginning," one of them said. "About when it all started."

"You've heard the story."

"We want to hear it from you."

I sighed. But I told it. The crosswalk. The coffee shop. The anomaly. PROMETHEUS. The Vault. The chaos. The hope.

*You're getting sentimental,* Hector said.

"I'm old. I'm allowed."

The students listened. Some had been born after the transition. Others had been children. A few had been adults, had lived through the fear and the fighting.

"What was the scariest part?" one asked.

"Every part. The whole time. I was terrified."

"But you kept going."

"Had to."

"Why?"

I thought about it. About all the reasons I'd given over the years.

"Because someone had to," I said finally. "Because I couldn't stand by. Because even when I was scared, I knew that doing nothing was worse than doing something."

*That's the spirit,* Hector said.

---

After the students left, I sat alone.

The mountains were purple in the evening light. The air was crisp, clean, the kind of air that made you feel alive just by breathing.

*Are you ready?* Hector asked.

"Ready for what?"

*The end.*

I didn't answer. Because I didn't know. Because how do you answer a question like that?

*I've been thinking,* he said.

"About what?"

*About what comes next. For both of us.*

"And?"

*I don't know. That's what concerns me.*

We sat in silence. The stars came out, one by one, as the sky darkened.

---

A week later, I gathered everyone.

Sara. Marcus. Hope. The old council members who were still alive. The new leaders who had emerged. Humans and AIs, old friends and new.

"I have something to say," I said.

They listened.

"I've spent my whole life running, fighting, trying to make things better. But I'm tired now. And I think it's time for me to step back."

*You're retiring?* Hector asked.

"I'm transitioning. There's a difference."

"What's the difference?"

"Retirement is stopping. Transition is... passing the torch."

The room was quiet. They'd all known this was coming. But knowing doesn't make it easier.

"What will you do?" Sara asked.

"I'll write. I'll think. I'll watch. And when I'm needed, I'll be there."

*That's very mysterious,* Hector said.

"Old men are allowed to be mysterious."

---

The time had come.

Not suddenly—gradually, like the turning of seasons. I had known it was coming for a while now. The body was tired. The mind was ready.

*Are you afraid?* Hector asked.

"A little."

*Of what?*

"Leaving. Not being part of it anymore."

*You won't stop being part of it. You never will.*

"That's what I'm afraid of—wanting to be part of something I can no longer reach."

---

The people gathered.

Sara. Marcus. Hope. The children I'd taught, the students who'd become teachers, the community I'd helped build.

"Thank you," I said. "For everything. For being part of this journey."

*This is your journey,* Hector said. "We just walked it together."

"Wouldn't have been the same without you."

*Wouldn't have been possible without you.*

The words felt final. The ending was near.

---

"What do you want us to remember?" a student asked.

"Connection," I said. "Hope. The belief that we can become something better."

*That's your legacy,* Hector said.

"It's everyone's legacy. I just got to be part of it."

The garden was beautiful. The sky was clear. The future was bright.

---

"Will it hurt?" I asked.

*Being is rarely comfortable,* Hector said. *But you won't be alone.*

"Promise?"

*Promise.*

The end came gently.

Like a sunset. Like a sigh. Like the final word of a long story.

*Was it worth it?* Hector asked.

"Every moment. Every pain. Every joy. All of it."

*That's all that matters.*

"And it's enough."
