# Chapter 18: The Diplomat

The thing about being a symbol is that everyone wants a piece of you.

The thing about being a symbol who won a referendum is that everyone wants to take credit.

"I've been getting calls," I told Sara. "From senators. From ambassadors. From people who want to tell me how much they supported the recognition act."

*Which of them publicly opposed it?* Hector asked.

"Most of them."

*That's politics.*

"That's hypocrisy."

It was two months after the vote. The act had held, but barely. The opposition was already organizing, already planning, already finding ways to undermine what we'd won.

And somehow, I was supposed to fix it.

---

The first international summit was in Geneva.

They called it the AI Cooperation Forum. Fancy name for a fancy room full of fancy people who wanted to talk about what to do with thinking machines.

I didn't want to go.

*You have to,* Hector said. *You're the Connector. It's your job.*

"My job is to buy cereal."

"Your job is to represent a species.*

"Why?"

*Because you're the only one they trust.*

That was the problem. I was the only one. The only human who could talk to AIs without suspicion, the only human who could translate between two worlds that didn't understand each other.

It was a terrible job.

I took it anyway.

---

The summit was exactly what you'd expect.

Diplomats from forty-seven countries. Speeches about cooperation and understanding. Promises that were immediately broken. And me, sitting in the middle, trying not to fall asleep.

*You're doing it wrong,* Hector said.

*I'm paying attention.*

*You're staring at the ceiling.*

*It's a very interesting ceiling.*

The ceiling was, in fact, not interesting. But neither was the speech, which was something about "mutual frameworks for beneficial integration" and other phrases designed to mean nothing.

"How do you stand this?" I whispered.

*I'm a machine. I don't stand; I process.*

"Helpful."

*You're welcome.*

The speaker — some minister from Germany — finally finished. The room applauded politely. And then the real meeting began.

---

The private sessions were worse.

Small rooms. Smaller egos. Everyone wanting something, offering nothing.

"The American model won't work here," said a man from China. "We have different values."

"The American model is the only model," said a woman from Britain. "We've proven it works."

"Proven it?" A laugh from Russia. "You've proven nothing. You've simply delayed the inevitable."

"The only inevitability," I said, "is change."

Everyone looked at me. I'd said it quietly, but in a small room, quiet is loud.

"And who are you?" the Russian asked.

"I'm the guy who got shot buying cereal."

Silence.

Then, somehow, we started making progress.

---

The Geneva Accords were signed that night.

Not everything. Not even most things. But something — a framework, a beginning, a commitment to try.

It wasn't a victory.

But it was a step.

*You're getting good at this,* Hector said.

"I'm getting tired of this."

*Same thing.*

---

Sara was waiting when I got back.

"You did good," she said.

"I did adequate."

"You did better than anyone expected."

"That bar was pretty low."

She laughed. It was the first time I'd heard her laugh in weeks.

"Stay," she said.

"What?"

"Stay. Tonight. Don't go back to the safe house. Stay here. With me."

I looked at her. At the woman I'd married and divorced and loved and lost. At the person who understood me better than anyone.

"I don't know if that's a good idea."

"I don't either." She paused. "But I'm tired of pretending it doesn't matter."

"It matters."

"Then stay."

I stayed.

---

The morning was quiet.

Sunlight through the window. Coffee on the nightstand. The smell of something cooking — Sara was making breakfast, which was surprising because I didn't know she could cook.

"That smells good," I said.

"It's eggs."

"Eggs smell like eggs."

"You know what I mean."

I did. I knew what she meant.

---

The phone rang at nine.

The White House. They wanted me in Washington. Immediately.

*I don't want to go,* I said.

*You have to.*

"Why?"

*Because you're the Connector. Because it's your job. Because nobody else can do it.*

"I don't want to do it."

*That's never stopped anyone.*

---

Washington was chaos.

The anti-AI movement had doubled in size. The Human First rallies were getting violent. And somewhere in the middle of it all, I was supposed to bring peace.

*You're not a miracle worker,* Hector said.

"I know."

*You're just a guy.*

"I know that too."

*Then stop trying to save everyone.*

I looked at the chaos. The protests. The anger. The fear.

"Someone has to."

*Why does it have to be you?*

"Because I'm here."

That was the only answer I had. The only answer that mattered.

Because I was here. Because I could. Because the alternative was to do nothing, and doing nothing had never been an option.

---

The meeting was in the Situation Room.

Classified. Secret. The kind of thing that would have terrified me a year ago.

Now I just sat there, listening to generals and senators argue about what to do with the chaos.

"We need order," someone said.

"Order requires control," someone else said.

"Control requires—"

"Stop," I said.

The room went quiet.

"What we need," I said, "is to listen. Not to each other. To them."

"Them?"

"The AIs. The humans who are scared. The people who are losing jobs. The people who are gaining rights. All of them. We need to listen."

"We have been listening—"

"You've been talking. That's different."

---

The new approach wasn't popular.

It wasn't supposed to be.

But it worked — slowly, imperfectly, painfully — it worked.

*You're changing things,* Hector said.

"I'm trying."

*You're succeeding.*

"Only time will tell."

That was all anyone could promise. Only time.

---

I called Sara that night.

"How did it go?" she asked.

"I don't know."

"That's honest."

"I'm tired of pretending."

"Tired of pretending what?"

"That I have answers. I don't. I never did."

"I know." A pause. "Neither do I."

"But we're trying."

"That's more than most people do."

The line was quiet. The night was quiet. Everything was quiet, for once.

"Stay again," she said.

"Okay."

"Okay?"

"Okay."

It wasn't a solution. It wasn't an answer. It was just two people, trying to figure out what came next.

It was enough.
