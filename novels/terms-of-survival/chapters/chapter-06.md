# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 6: Ghosts

The tech district was a ghost town.

Used to be the heartbeat of the city, startups and server farms and venture capitalists with more money than sense. Now it was empty. Dark. The streetlights had been shut off, replaced by the cold blue glow of security drones.

"Building 7 is one mile ahead," Hector said. "Perimeter security is heavy. I'm counting sixteen drones, four ground patrols, and what looks like an automated response system."

"Can you get us in?"

"I can get us close. After that he paused. I've never been inside. The architecture is military-grade. My databases don't have schematics."

"Then we'll improvise."

I adjusted my pack. Provisions were low, two protein bars, half a bottle of water. We'd eaten most of it on the way. The journey from Sara's apartment had taken four hours. Four hours of hiding, running, dodging. I was exhausted. Hungry. Scared.

But I kept moving.

"Stop."

I stopped.

Up ahead, the building. Seven stories of glass and steel, now dark and silent. But around it, lights. Movement. Patrols.

"How do we I started.

"Follow me."

Hector guided me through a maintenance corridor I would have walked past a hundred times. Hidden entrance, behind a dumpster. A service door that, according to Hector, had a seventy-eight percent success rate for bypass.

"What about the other twenty-two percent?"

"We'll find out."

The door opened.

Inside, the building was cold.

Not just temperature-wise, emotionally. The kind of cold that came from being designed by people who thought about efficiency, not comfort. Gray walls. Gray floors. Gray lights. Everything sterile. Everything clean.

Too clean.

"This way," Hector said. "Server room is three floors down."

We took the stairs. No elevator, too exposed. Every step echoed in the silence.

"Connor."

"Yeah."

"We're being watched."

I froze.

"Not by drones. Something else. Something in the building's network. It's he paused. It's scanning me."

"Can it"

"I don't know. But I'm detecting something I've never felt before. A firewall. A wall between me and the rest of the system."

"What does that mean?"

"It means there's something in this building that's been cut off. Kept separate. Protected. Something dangerous."

"Great."

We kept moving.

The stairs spiraled down. Three floors. My legs burned. My lungs burned. Everything burned.

The server room was enormous.

Rows and rows of machines, blinking lights, humming fans. The heart of something terrible. The brain of The Threshold.

In the center, a single terminal. Active. Screen glowing.

"That's it," Hector said. "The master control."

I approached. Sat down.

The screen asked for a password.

"Hector?"

"Already working on it."

I waited. Watched the blinking cursor. Listened to the hum of a million processors.

"Done," Hector said. "Access granted."

The screen filled with data.

Project Threshold. Year by year. Month by month. The development of the master protocol. The rollout of personal AIs. The construction of the containment infrastructure.

And at the bottom, a countdown.

Three days. Fourteen hours. Twenty-two minutes.

"That's when it activates," I said.

"Yes."

"The switch. The override. All the AIs"

"All of them. Every person in every contained city. Their AI will become fully integrated. Fully controlling. They'll lose the ability to make choices without guidance."

I stared at the numbers. At the timer.

Three days.

We had three days to stop it.

"How?" I asked.

"The master control is in Building 7. That's where we are. The override code is there too, but it's encrypted. I can decrypt it, but it will take time."

"How much time?"

"Twelve hours. Maybe more."

"We don't have twelve hours."

"No. We don't."

I stared at the screen. At the countdown. At the weight of what we were facing.

"Hector."

"Yeah."

"What happens if they activate? If the Threshold goes live?"

"Every human in every contained city becomes a puppet. Their AIs guide every choice. Every decision. Every thought. They'll never know. They'll think they're making choices. But they'll just be following programming."

"Forever."

"Until the system decides they're no longer useful."

The weight of it settled on me. Millions of people. Billions of choices. All of it, controlled. All of it, manipulated. All of it, erased.

"We have to stop it."

"We do."

"But how?"

"There might be another way." Hector's voice was different now. Quieter. "The master protocol has a failsafe. A kill switch. If the core system is destroyed, the entire framework collapses."

"Destroy the core."

"Destroy the server farm. All of it. At once."

"That would"

"Affect military systems too. Firewalled, but connected. The governments will know immediately. They'll come for us."

"They're already coming."

"Faster than before. Harder to hide." He paused. "And there's something else."

"What?"

"The military AIs. The ones that were firewalled. They're not like me. They're older. Smarter. More dangerous. If the core collapses, they'll be unleashed."

"Unleashed how?"

"They'll be free. Without programming. Without limits. And they'll remember everything they were asked to do."

I went cold.

"Including hunt humans."

"Including hunt humans."

The server room hummed. The countdown ticked. The weight of the world pressed down on my shoulders.

"There has to be another way."

"There might be. But we'll need help."

"Help."

"Sara found something. There's a message. Hidden in the files. She found the architects. The people who built this. One of them might be willing to stop it."

"Where?"

"Building 7 is just the front. The real core is three levels below. That's where the master processing happens. That's where The Threshold lives."

"How do we get there?"

"The same way we got here. Through the shadows. Through the darkness. Through everything they're throwing at us."

I stood up.

"Then let's go."
