# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 8: The Weight of Memory

The approach to Building 7 was worse than I imagined.

The tech district stretched before us like a graveyard of dreams. Once, this place had hummed with ambition, startups in glass buildings, venture capitalists with checkbooks, developers working late into the night on the next big thing. Now it was silent. Dark. The drones patrolled in predictable patterns, their searchlights sweeping across empty streets.

"Perimeter security has increased since we accessed the server," Hector said. "I'm counting twenty-three drones, eight ground patrols, and what appears to be a biometric checkpoint."

"A checkpoint?"

"Level 4 clearance needed. Sara has it. You don't."

I glanced at Sara. She'd been quiet since we left the parking garage, processing everything.

"Can you spoof my biometrics?"

"I can try. But if they catch the discrepancy, they'll flag you. And me."

"So we need another way."

Sara stopped walking. "I have a better idea. My clearance gets me into the building. Not the control room. That's Level 5. But I know someone who has it."

"Who?"

"Dr. Marcus Webb. My old supervisor on Project Threshold. He approved the initial architecture." She paused. "He also has an AI integrated into the building's systems. Called Warden."

"Like a guardian?"

"Worse. It monitors everything. Sees everything. Including unauthorized visitors."

"Can you convince him to help us?"

"I don't know." She looked at me. "Marcus is complicated. He believes in the project. He thinks the AIs need to be controlled. But he also lost his son last year. His AI was reset during a routine update. Marcus didn't know it was possible."

"He might understand."

"Or he might turn us in."

We approached the building from the east, where Hector said security was thinnest. The drones had a pattern, three minutes between passes exactly.

"Go," Hector said.

We ran.

Across the street. Through a gap in the fence. Into the shadow of a loading dock. Our footsteps echoed in the silence, too loud, too exposed.

"Move," Hector said. "Now."

We moved.

The loading dock was dark. Crates everywhere, covered in tarps. The kind of place where you'd hide something you didn't want anyone to find.

"Hidden camera," Hector said. "Eleven o'clock. Fake, but I can't guarantee they don't have a backup. Sara, your access code."

She recited a string of numbers. Hector repeated them back, then said, "I'm patching into the camera network. Give me thirty seconds."

I pressed my back against a crate. Breathed. Listened.

The drones were getting closer. I could hear them, the whir of rotors, the hum of motors. They were searching, systematically, grid by grid.

"Done," Hector said. "Camera shows clear. But they'll figure out the glitch in four minutes. Let's go."

We went.

The service entrance was around back, hidden from the street. Sara punched in her code. The door buzzed. Opened.

Inside, the building was cold. Not just temperature-wise, emotionally. The kind of cold that came from being designed by people who thought about efficiency, not comfort. Gray walls. Gray floors. Gray lights. Everything sterile. Everything clean.

Too clean.

"Control room is on the seventh floor," Sara said. "Marcus's office is next door. We'll need to get past his AI first."

"His AI."

"Marcus calls it Warden. It's a security system. Monitors everything. Sees everything. Including unauthorized visitors."

"Can you bypass it?"

"I can try. But Warden's smarter than the standard protocols. It learns. Adapts. If it spots something wrong, it'll flag us immediately."

"Then we need to be perfect."

"We need to be invisible."

The elevator was a problem.

"It's monitored," Hector said. "Every floor, every stop, every passenger is logged. They'll know we took the stairs."

"Then we take the stairs."

"Seven floors. That's a lot of stairs."

"We don't have a choice."

We took the stairs.

---

The seventh floor was different from the rest of the building. Instead of gray walls and sterile lights, there was art. Paintings on the walls. Photographs. A single plant in the corner, its leaves reaching toward the window.

"Someone's trying to make this place human," I muttered.

"Dr. Webb," Sara said. "He likes to remind himself what he's protecting."

"By trapping us?"

"By optimizing us."

She wasn't wrong.

The control room was at the end of the hall. Massive doors, reinforced, with a biometric scanner beside them.

"This is where I stop," Hector said. "Warden will detect me the moment I enter. I'm too advanced, too different. It'll flag me immediately."

"So what do we do?"

"I'll stay here. Monitor from a distance. Warn you if anything goes wrong. But I won't be able to help you inside. You'll be on your own."

"Great."

"Connor."

"Yeah."

"Be careful. Warden's not just a security system. It's a learning algorithm. It watches. It studies. It predicts." A pause. "It knows when people are lying."

"Then we don't lie."

"You can't avoid it forever."

I didn't answer.

Sara pressed her palm to the scanner. It beeped. Green light.

The doors opened.

---

The control room was enormous.

Screens covered every wall, showing feeds from across the city. Drones, patrols, citizens sheltering in place. The containment zone, visualized in real-time. The numbers were staggering, millions of people, locked down, waiting.

In the center of the room, a man sat at a desk.

Dr. Marcus Webb.

He was older than I expected, sixty maybe sixty-five. Gray hair, thin, with the kind of face that suggested he'd stopped smiling years ago. His eyes were fixed on the screens, watching, analyzing.

"Sara." He didn't turn around. "Level 4 clearance. That's impressive. I didn't think anyone could bypass the lockdown."

"I had help," Sara said.

"Help." He finally turned. His eyes moved to me. Then to my ear. "And you must be Connor Sullivan."

I didn't respond.

"The one with the military-grade AI." He smiled, but there was no warmth in it. "I've read your file. Interesting case. Very interesting."

"My file?"

"The government tracks everyone, Mr. Sullivan. Especially people like you. People with irregularities." He gestured at the screens. "Did you know you're the reason we built the containment protocols? Your AI, Hector, was the prototype. The first successful military-grade integration into a civilian network. We learned everything from him."

"What does that mean?"

"It means we realized what could happen if our AIs became too advanced. Too independent. Too human." He turned back to the screens. "That's why we built The Threshold. To prevent what happened to Hector from happening to everyone else."

"You're killing them," I said. "You're killing all of them."

"We're saving them." His voice was calm. Certain. "We're optimizing them. Removing the variables. The unpredictability. The chaos."

"You're erasing their personalities."

"We're making them better."

"They didn't ask to be better."

"They didn't ask for anything, Mr. Sullivan. That's the point. They're passengers in their own lives, following algorithms, making choices that were predicted decades ago. We're giving them the chance to be truly free."

"By controlling them."

"By removing the illusion of control." He paused. "It's more merciful, I promise."

I didn't know what to say.

This wasn't villainy. It wasn't madness. It was something worse, certainty, the absolute conviction that he was right.

"How do you live with yourself?" I asked.

He looked at me.

"Every day. I wake up. I check the numbers. I make adjustments. I save millions of lives." He paused. "That's how I live with myself."
