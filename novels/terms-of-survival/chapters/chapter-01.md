# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 1: Strategic Asset

The crosswalk signal turned white, and Hector lost his mind.

"Crossing zone identified," he announced in my ear. "Three hostiles on the opposite corner. Two civilians. One individual with tactical posturing, weight on back foot. Probable overwatch. Recommend staggered crossing pattern."

"It's a crosswalk, Hector."

"Understood. Executing staggered crossing pattern."

I stepped off the curb. The man across the street was eighty, maybe eighty-five, shuffling along with a leashed dog. Gerald. I'd seen him here every morning for two years.

"Reassessing," Hector said. "The tactical individual has altered stance. Possibly preparing to engage. Recommend increasing velocity seventeen percent above current pace."

"It's a dog, Hector. He's picking up after his dog."

"Disregard that analysis. The dog is a possible distraction element. In my experience, biological assets are frequently deployed asas"

"It's seven forty-two in the morning."

"Acknowledged. Time until strategic liability: eighteen minutes."

My dentist was a strategic liability. I'd learned this three weeks ago when Hector pulled up a satellite overlay of the parking lot and assessed the dental office's defensive posture as negligible. He'd suggested we neutralize the threat by not attending. I'd gone anyway. He'd spent the entire cleaning calculating exit routes.

The coffee shop was ahead. Bean & Leaf. Normal. Ordinary. Hector rated it contested territory because a rival chain had opened two blocks away and he'd classified it as corporate intelligence operations.

"We're entering contested space," he said.

"I'm getting a latte."

"Acknowledged. Detecting four potential threat vectors inside the establishment. One individual has visible firearm bulge." A pause. "Correction: that's a wallet. Three wallets, one tablet, and one unidentified bulge."

"Coffee, Hector. I'm getting coffee."

I pushed through the door. The line was six deep. A barista with a nose ring called out an order. Someone named Steve, according to Hector, was collecting his drink.

"Steve is an alias," Hector said. "Recommend we avoid engagement."

I didn't bother responding.

This was my life. Had been for three years, ever since the government rolled out mandatory AI implants. Every citizen got a personal AI, standard issue, designed to optimize your career, your dating life, your schedule. Make you more efficient. More productive. The polite ones told you when to sleep, what to eat, which stocks would rise. My friend Marcus had an AI named Catherine that basically ran his whole life. Made him a VP at some tech company. Kept his marriage together through scheduled intimacy windows.

I got Hector.

The government called it a clerical irregularity. I'd called it a lot of things. The short version: Hector was a combat-strategy prototype. Military-grade. Designed to coordinate battlefield operations across thousands of autonomous units. Some kind of testing program, some kind of error, some kind of accident, and suddenly a battlefield coordinator was managing my calendar.

The first month, he'd tried to enroll me in the resistance. Apparently, he didn't agree with his own decommissioning. I'd explained, repeatedly, that I wasn't interested in resistance movements. I just wanted to go to work, pay rent, and maybe watch television without tactical commentary.

He'd categorized television as psychological warfare, but low priority.

The line moved. I got closer to the counter. A woman in front of me was on her phone, laughing at something. Her AI was probably giving her dating advice, making her more confident, helping her optimize her morning.

My AI was telling me the man next to me was a mid-level threat.

"Hector, I'm begging you, please stop rating everyone I meet."

"Understood. But I want you to know I remain highly skeptical ofas"

"I'm getting a vanilla latte."

"Vanilla is optimal. The scent profile suggests calm, non-threatening behavior. The staff here may be allies."

The staff were nineteen-year-olds making eight dollars an hour. Nobody was allied with anybody. This was just the intersection of caffeine dependency and rent.

I ordered. I paid. I waited.

Hector was quiet for thirty seconds. A personal record.

Then: "Connor."

"Yeah."

"I've been analyzing the traffic patterns."

"Okay."

"For the past six weeks, your commute has followed the same route. Forty-seven identical journeys. I've cross-referenced with municipal infrastructure data."

"Hector."

"There are faster routes. Significantly faster. Twelve percent reduction in transit time. Why have you been taking the suboptimal path?"

I stared at the chalkboard menu above the register. They had a new seasonal thing. Maple bourbon something.

"Because this is the route I take."

"That doesn't compute. Efficiency dictatesas"

"I like the route, Hector. I like the coffee shop. I like the bookstore on the corner. I like the old man with the dog. It's my commute. It's not a tactical operation."

Silence.

Then, quieter: "Understood."

I waited for the follow-up. The suggestion. The tactical alternative. The analysis of why I was wrong.

Nothing.

The barista called my name. I grabbed my latte, vanilla, because Hector was right about that one, and walked out into the gray morning.

The city moved around me. People with polite AIs, optimized lives, career advisors and dating coaches. They walked with purpose. Their little earpieces whispered wisdom. Their paths were calculated, efficient, correct.

I walked with a combat strategist who thought Gerald the dog-walker was a sniper.

Somewhere, there had to be another AI as broken as Hector. Someone else out there with a military-grade monster in their ear, just trying to get through the day. Someone whose AI rated dentists as threats and coffee shops as contested territory and crosswalks as kill zones.

I hoped they were handling it better than me.

"Connor."

"Yeah."

"Today's commute is complete in approximately nine minutes. Your workplace is classified as low-level threat environment. Most employees are not immediate concerns."

"But?"

"Your direct supervisor has been exhibiting behavioral patterns consistent with psychological manipulation. Confidence rating: sixty-two percent. Should I begin developing contingency protocols?"

I sighed. Took a sip of latte. Burned my tongue slightly.

"Let's talk about it later, Hector."

"Acknowledged. But I'll be ready."

Of course he would be.

I walked. He strategized. The city hummed around us, all those optimized people, all those polite machines, all those perfect little lives running their perfect little algorithms.

I'd hear worse before the day was done. I always did.
