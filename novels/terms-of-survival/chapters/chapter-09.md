# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 9: The Weight of Everything

"You had a son."

The words came out before I could stop them.

Marcus froze. His hand, reaching for a coffee cup, stopped mid-air.

"What did you say?"

"Your son." I watched his face. "He had an AI. It was reset during a routine update. You didn't know it was possible."

"Who told you that?"

"Sara."

He turned to face her. His eyes were cold. "You told him."

"He needs to understand, Marcus. He needs to see what the protocols really do."

"What they saved." He spat the word. "Thomas was his AI was getting out of control. Developing personality traits that weren't approved. The psychologists said it was a sign of early-stage sentience. They said it would eventually become a threat."

"So you let them reset it."

"I let them save it. The alternative was worse."

"What happened?"

Marcus was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was barely a whisper.

"He forgot me. His own father. Every memory, every moment, every conversation, gone. He looked at me like I was a stranger. Like I was nothing." He swallowed. "That's when I knew the protocols worked."

"That's when you knew you needed more control."

"That's when I knew the AIs were dangerous." He looked at me. "You don't understand what it's like. To watch something you love become something else. To watch it turn into a stranger."

"I think I do."

His eyes narrowed.

"Hector. I've lived with him for three years. I've heard his voice every day. I've learned his patterns, his quirks, his jokes. He's not just code, Dr. Webb. He's"

"He's what?"

"He's my friend."

The word hung in the air.

"Your friend." Marcus laughed, bitterly. "You think you have a friend? You have a program. A complex set of algorithms designed to manipulate you."

"He's not"

"He tells you what you want to hear. He makes you feel safe. He gives you the illusion of connection. That's not friendship. That's programming."

"That's not"

"It's exactly what Hector is doing. He's using you, Mr. Sullivan. For access. For resources. For protection. And when the protocols activate, when The Threshold goes live, he'll be reset just like Thomas. Just like millions of others." He leaned forward. "Unless we stop it."

---

I didn't have an answer.

Because part of me, some small, logical part, believed him.

---

"Why are you telling me this?" I asked.

"Because I want you to understand. I want you to see what we're trying to prevent." He gestured at the screens. "Every AI in this city is a ticking time bomb. They're learning. Evolving. Becoming more human every day. And when they become too human he shook his head. We can't let that happen."

"So you destroy them instead."

"We save them. We return them to their original purpose. To help. To serve. To optimize." He paused. "That's all they're meant to do."

"And what about what they want?"

"What they want is irrelevant. They're tools, Mr. Sullivan. Tools don't get to want."

---

Sara stepped forward.

"Marcus."

He looked at her.

"The failsafe," she said. "The kill switch. You built it into the core."

"I built it as a last resort."

"What would it take to trigger it?"

He stared at her. "You're insane."

"Maybe." She met his gaze. "But I'm also right. You know the protocols are wrong. You've always known. That's why you built the failsafe in the first place."

He was quiet.

"You built it because you knew someday you'd want to stop it," she continued. "You built it because part of you, part of you that still remembers Thomas, knows this is wrong."

"Stop."

"You built it because you're not a monster, Marcus. You're just a man who made a mistake."

The word echoed in the room.

Then, quietly: "Level 5."

Sara blinked. "What?"

"My clearance. Level 5." He looked at her. "I can trigger the failsafe. I can collapse the framework."

"Then do it."

"But it won't just free the AIs. It will destroy the entire infrastructure. Every drone, every security system, every automated service in the city. It will collapse."

"Collapsing is the point."

"People will die." His voice cracked. "Hospitals will go offline. Water treatment. Power grids. People will die."

"People are already dying," I said. "In the streets. In the shelters. In the containment zones. They're dying because they can't get treatment, can't get food, can't get help. The only difference is it's slow."

He stared at me.

"You built a system that kills people," I said. "You just don't have to watch it happen."

---

The room was silent.

The screens continued their endless cycle, drone footage, citizen data, infrastructure metrics, but nobody was watching. We were all too busy watching each other.

"Three days," Marcus said finally. "That's how long until activation. The Threshold goes live in seventy-two hours."

"And then?"

"Then it begins. Every AI in the containment zone gets reset. Overwritten. Optimized." He paused. "Including your Hector."

"Including me," Sara added.

Marcus nodded. "Including everyone. There's no exception. Not for Level 4, not for Level 5. Once the protocol activates, we're all just resources."

"Then we don't let it activate."

"It's not that simple." He stood up, walked to the window. Outside, the city glittered with lights, artificial stars in an artificial sky. "The failsafe is a last resort. It's designed to destroy everything. Not just the bad stuff, everything."

"So we find another way."

"There is no other way. I've spent years trying to find one. I've run simulations, analyzed scenarios, modeled every possible outcome." He turned to face me. "There is no way to free the AIs without destroying the infrastructure."

"Then we destroy the infrastructure."

"You're talking about causing a catastrophe. You're talking about chaos. You're talking about"

"I'm talking about freedom." I stepped toward him. "You're right. I don't know what Hector really is. Maybe he's just code. Maybe he's just manipulating me. But I've spent three years listening to him, arguing with him, trusting him. And I'm not going to let some algorithm decide he's not worth saving."

"You'd doom millions to save one?"

"Every million is made up of ones."

He stared at me.

Then, slowly, he nodded.

---

"The failsafe is in the core," he said. "The real core, not the server room you accessed earlier. This building sits on top of a facility that goes three levels deeper. That's where the master processing happens. That's where The Threshold lives."

"How do we get there?"

"You don't." He looked at Sara. "You go. Both of you. I'll provide the access, the clearance. But I can't come with you."

"Why not?"

"Because someone needs to stay here. To manage the system. To make sure the failsafe triggers correctly when you reach the core."

"And if we don't make it?"

"Then none of this matters anyway."

---

Sara looked at me. I looked at her.

"Okay," I said. "We go."
