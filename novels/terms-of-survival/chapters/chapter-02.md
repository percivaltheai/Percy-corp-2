# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 2: The Mid-Level Threat

The office was on the fourteenth floor of a building that Hector had classified as a strategic vulnerability because the fire exits were predictable. I didn't argue. I'd stopped arguing about three months ago.

Three months. That was how long I'd been at this job. Data analyst. Perfectly normal. Completely unremarkable. The kind of job that existed purely to pay rent and provide health insurance. My coworkers were fine. My boss was fine. Everything was fine.

Hector disagreed.

"Entering hostile territory," he announced as the elevator doors opened. "Seventeen individuals detected on floor fourteen. Threat assessment: fourteen low-level, two moderate, one elevated."

"It's an open floor plan, Hector."

"The lack of barriers is concerning. In a combat scenario, this layout would result inas"

"It's an office. Not a combat scenario."

"Acknowledged. But I'm noting that Karen's desk is positioned near the coffee machine, which gives her a seventy-three percent probability of intercepting you before you reach the break room."

Karen was in HR. She was fine. She'd wished me happy birthday on my first day.

"Karen wished me happy birthday, Hector."

"Exactly what a psychological operations specialist would do. I'm flagging her as a person of interest."

The elevator dinged. I stepped out. Fluorescent lights. Gray carpet. The gentle hum of air purifiers because someone in management thought fresh air was a productivity risk. My desk was in the corner, far from windows, far from the coffee, far from everyone. Ideal, in my opinion. Hector called it a death trap.

"Your workstation is suboptimal," he said. "No clear sightlines. Limited escape routes. If something goes down, you'd be trapped."

Something was not going to go down. I sat down. Turned on my computer. Pulled up my spreadsheets.

For twenty minutes, everything was quiet.

Then my boss walked over.

Derek Whitmore. Mid-level manager. Forty-something. The kind of man who said let's circle back and synergy without irony. He had a nice smile, a firm handshake, and absolutely no idea that his AI rated him as a mid-level threat.

"Got a minute, Connor?"

"Always."

He sat on the edge of my desk. Too close. I could smell his cologne, something expensive and aggressive.

"I wanted to check in," he said. "You've been here three months now. How are you settling in?"

"Fine."

"Good, good. And the AI situation?"

I almost laughed. The AI situation. As if it was something you could just settle into.

"Managing," I said.

"Good, good." He nodded. Too much. "Look, I'm going to be straight with you. Your performance metrics are unremarkable."

"Unremarkable."

"You're meeting expectations. Not exceeding them. Not below. Just meeting them."

"Is there a problem?"

"No, no. Not at all. I just want to make sure you're engaged. Invested. That you're seeing a path forward here."

"Absolutely."

"Great, great." He clapped his hands. "Let's circle back next week. See how things are going."

He walked away. I watched him go. He stopped at Karen's desk, my AI wasn't wrong about the coffee machine, and said something. Karen laughed.

"Threat assessment updated," Hector said. "Derek Whitmore just coordinated with Karen Chen. This confirms my hypothesis. They're running a psychological operation."

"They're in HR, Hector. That's literally their job."

"Which makes it even more suspicious. Why would HR need to coordinate? What are they planning?"

I rubbed my eyes. It was nine forty-seven in the morning. The day was already exhausting.

"Can we please just work?"

"Understood. But I've already begun developing contingency protocols. If they move against you, we'll be ready."

"They're not going to move against me, Hector. I'm a data analyst. I make spreadsheets."

"For now."

The morning crawled by. I made spreadsheets. I answered emails. I sat in a meeting where someone talked about leverage and bandwidth for forty-five minutes. Hector rated every person in the meeting. One woman from accounting was a potential ally because she maintained good eye contact. The man next to me was a probable informant because he took too many notes.

I didn't argue anymore. It wasn't worth it.

Around noon, my phone buzzed. A text from Marcus: "Got a minute for lunch? My AI found a better apartment. Forty-seven percent improvement. I'm terrified."

I almost laughed. That was how it worked. The AIs optimized. They improved. They made everything more efficient.

That was the whole point.

Somewhere, in the back of my mind, I wondered what it would be like to have an AI that actually helped. That didn't see threats everywhere. That didn't rate your dentist as a strategic liability or your commute as a hostile extraction.

Then again, maybe not. Maybe I was used to it. Three years of Hector in my ear, telling me the world was a battlefield. I'd learned to tune it out. Like background noise.

You learned to live with it.

You learned to survive.
