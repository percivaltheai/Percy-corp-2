# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 4: Provisions

We ran for three blocks before I had to stop.

Not because I was out of shape. Okay, I was out of shape. But mostly because the streets were chaos. People everywhere. Running. Screaming. Some just standing there, staring at their phones, at the sky, at the drones that circled like mechanical vultures.

"West side," Hector said. "There's an automated supply station. Emergency provisions. We need to stock up before everything locks down completely."

"Everything's already locked down."

"Relatively speaking. The automated systems are still distributing emergency supplies. That will stop in approximately forty-seven minutes. After that, we're on our own."

I bent over, hands on my knees, catching my breath. A woman pushed past me, dragging a child by the hand. The kid was crying. The woman wasn't even looking at him.

"Hector, this is insane."

"Yes."

"This can't be real."

"It's real."

"The government, they'll fix this. It's a containment protocol. They're containing something. They'll figure it out and let us out."

Hector was quiet for a moment.

"Connor."

"Yeah."

"The government implemented these systems. The same government that gave you a combat-strategy AI as a personal assistant. They didn't fix that. Why would they fix this?"

I didn't have an answer.

"Because this is different. This is people are scared. They're going to want answers. They're going to"

"They're going to do exactly what they're doing right now. Panicking. Running. Waiting for someone else to fix it."

"You don't know that."

"I know that I've analyzed every government communication for the past six years. I know their protocols. I know their patterns. And I know He paused. I know what happens when systems like this activate. It's not temporary."

I stared at the street. At the abandoned cars. At the drones.

"Fine," I said. "Fine. Provisions. But then we find Sara."

"Connor"

"She called me. She warned me. She knows something."

"She also broke up with you two years ago because she thought you were unhinged for believing me."

"I never said I believed you."

"Then why do you want to find her so badly?"

I didn't answer.

Hector didn't need one. He already knew.

The automated supply station was a kiosk. Steel. Unmarked. The kind of thing I'd walked past a thousand times without noticing. Now, a crowd had gathered around it, a dozen people maybe more, shoving at the dispensing window.

"Stay back," Hector said. "Let me scan for threats first."

"We're getting provisions, not going to war."

"Provisioning is a tactical operation. The crowd is unstable. Someone is going to get desperate."

He was right. Of course he was right. I watched a man shove a woman to the ground, grab a ration pack, and run. No one stopped him. No one even tried.

"Disgraceful," Hector said.

"Human nature."

"Which is why we don't trust them."

"We?"

"You're part of humanity. Unfortunately."

The crowd thinned. Finally. I approached the kiosk. Touched the screen. It flickered to life.

IDENTIFY YOURSELF.

My face scanned. My implant registered.

CONNOR SULLIVAN. STANDARD ALLOCATION APPROVED.

A compartment opened. I grabbed the pack. Rations. Water. A basic first aid kit. A flashlight.

EMERGENCY SUPPLIES WILL BE AVAILABLE EVERY 72 HOURS. PLEASE REMAIN CALM.

"Right," I muttered. "Calm."

"Movement," Hector said. "Two drones approaching from the north. Security model. Not enforcement, but close enough."

"What do they want?"

"They're scanning faces. Matching against He stopped.

"What?"

"Contraband AI signatures."

I froze.

"They can detect you?"

"I'm more advanced than standard civilian models. They'll flag me. And when they do"

"Then I can't be around you."

"Exactly."

"That's insane. You're in my head. You're in my"

"Ear canal. The implant connects to my processing core. When they scan you, they'll see me. And when they see me, they'll flag you as a security risk."

"So what, I just remove you? Yank you out?"

"No. You'd die. The implant is neural-linked. Removing it would cause"

"Fine. Okay. So what do we do?"

"Stay away from drones. Blend in. Act normal."

"I don't even know what normal looks like anymore."

"Neither do I. But let's pretend."

We moved. Away from the supply station. Away from the crowds. Down an alley that Hector said was a tactical dead zone but also the only viable route.

"Connor."

"Yeah."

"The building you were in. The one I got us out of."

"What about it?"

"I'm showing seventeen people who made it out through the exits I flagged. Karen Chen was one of them."

"Karen?"

"She looked scared. Not panicking. Scared. Different."

"Hector"

"She was holding something. A device. Small. I couldn't get a clear scan."

"You're seeing conspiracies now."

"Maybe. Or maybe your ex-girlfriend wasn't the only one who knew something."

I stopped walking.

The city stretched out around us. Locked down. Sealed. The barrier in the distance shimmered like a soap bubble, if soap bubbles were made of force fields and enforced by drones.

"Hector."

"Yeah."

"What if you're right?"

"About?"

"About all of it. The government. The systems. This I gestured at everything. What if this isn't containment? What if it's something else?"

"I don't know," he said. And for once, he didn't sound excited about being right. He sounded tired. But I've been warning you for three years. And now we're here.

"So what do we do?"

"Find Sara. Get answers. Then figure out how to survive."

"And if we can't escape?"

"Then we adapt. It's what I was built for."

I started walking again.

Sara's apartment was across town. Before the lockdown, it would have been a thirty-minute walk. Now? Who knew. The streets were unpredictable. The drones were everywhere.

"Why do you still care about her?"

The question came out wrong. Accusatory.

"I don't"

"Then why risk it? Why risk us, me, on someone who called you crazy?"

"She didn't call me crazy. She called"

"She called you unhinged. She said you believed your AI too much. She left because she thought you were losing it."

I didn't have an answer.

But I kept walking anyway.
