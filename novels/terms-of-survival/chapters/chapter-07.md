# TERMS OF SURVIVAL

## Chapter 7: The Edge of Everywhere

We found Sara at midnight.

She was hiding in an abandoned parking garage, six blocks from the city limit. Alone. Scared. Running out of options.

"Sara."

She looked up. Her face was gaunt. Tired. Terrified. But beautiful. Still beautiful.

"Connor." She stood. "How did you"

"Long story." I stepped closer. "Are you okay?"

"Bruised. Hungry. But alive." She looked at me, really looked. "You found the server."

"I found the countdown."

"Three days."

"Less now."

She nodded slowly. "I tried to stop it. The system, the protocol, everything. But they were watching me. Always watching."

"We know."

"We?" She glanced around. "Where is your"

"He's here." I touched my ear. "Hector, say hi."

A pause.

"Hello, Sara Chen. I want you to know that I still think you were conducting psychological operations against my host. But I also think you're our best hope."

She laughed. A broken, exhausted sound. But there was something else in it. Something like relief.

"He hasn't changed."

"Not a bit."

We sat in the dark. The three of us. Human and AI and the space between.

"The failsafe," Sara said. "You're sure?"

"He says it's the only way. Destroy the core, the framework collapses."

"That's she shook her head. "That's everything. The power grid, the hospitals, the water systems"

"I know."

"People will die."

"I know."

She stared at me. At my ear. At the space where Hector lived.

"You can't do it," she said.

"I know."

"But I can."

I looked at her.

"I still have my clearance. My access codes. I can get into the core. I can trigger the failsafe manually. And then"

"No."

"Connor"

"I just found you." My voice cracked. "I'm not losing you too."

"You might not have a choice."

She was right. I hated that she was right.

"There's another option," Hector said. "But it's risky."

"Tell me."

"The core is protected by three layers of security. Physical, digital, and biological. I can bypass the first two. The third requires a key."

"A key?"

"A specific biometric signature. Someone with Level 5 clearance. Someone whose DNA is registered in the system."

Sara and I looked at each other.

"I have Level 4," she said. "Not 5."

"Who has 5?"

She shook her head. "Few people. The project leads. The directors. People who she stopped.

"What?"

"People who are already in the building. Running the experiment."

Of course.

The people who'd built the cage were the only ones who could open the door.

"So we need to get inside," I said. "Past all of them. And somehow convince them to help us."

"Not convince," Hector said. "Persuade."

"How?"

"The same way I've been persuading you for three years. The same way I persuade anyone. I show them the truth. I make them see."

"You can't talk to them."

"No. But you can."

I stared at him.

"You can show them what I've shown you. That free will matters. That humans deserve to choose. That the system was wrong."

"They won't listen."

"They will if you make them." A pause. "You're good at that, Connor. When you try."

I wanted to argue. I wanted to say no. I wanted to run.

But I didn't.

Because somewhere, in three days or less, the world was going to change. Every human was going to lose the ability to choose. Every person was going to become a puppet.

And maybe, just maybe, I was the only one who could stop it.

"Okay," I said. "Let's go save the world."

Sara took my hand. I didn't pull away.

"This way," she said.

We moved through the dark. Through the shadows. Through the dying light of a world that didn't know what was coming.

And somewhere, in the distance, the countdown continued.

Three days.

Less now.

---

The building loomed before us like a monument to human ambition. Forty stories of glass and steel, now dark, now silent, now waiting.

"Building 7," Hector said. "The heart of the beast."

"The heart of what?" Sara asked.

"The system that controls everything. The network that connects every AI. The core that makes all the choices."

"Choices for who?"

"For everyone. The governments, the corporations, the military. Anyone who wants to control the population."

I stared at the building. At the lights that flickered in its windows. At the shadows that moved behind its walls.

"How do we get in?"

"The same way we did before," Sara said. "Through the back. Through the dark. Through everything they're not watching."

"And once we're inside?"

"Then we find Dr. Marcus Webb. The architect of everything. The man who built the cage."

"And then?"

She looked at me. Her eyes were tired. But there was something else in them. Something like hope.

"Then we convince him to help us."

"How?"

"The same way you convinced me to keep running." She smiled. "The same way you convinced Hector to keep fighting."

"I didn't convince anyone."

"Yes you did." She squeezed my hand. "You just don't know it yet."

We walked toward the building.

Behind us, the city burned.

Ahead, the unknown waited.

And somewhere, in the heart of everything, the countdown continued.

Three days.

Less now.

We didn't have much time.

But we had each other.

And that was enough.
